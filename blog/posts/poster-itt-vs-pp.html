Intention-to-Treat vs. Per-Protocol
Two estimands, one dataset — when the choice matters and what to do about it
Alex Mourer — Causal Inference Methods
Hernán & Hernández-Díaz 2012 · Hernán & Robins 2017 · Murray et al. 2019
Introduction
Definitions
Intention-to-treat (ITT) effect

The effect of being assigned to a treatment strategy, regardless of the treatment actually received during follow-up.

Per-protocol (PP) effect

The effect of receiving the assigned treatment throughout follow-up, exactly as specified in the study protocol.

Causal structure

The causal effect of assigned treatment A depends on: the effect of received treatment Z (Z→Y); the degree of adherence (A→Z); and concurrent behavioural changes (A→Y, dashed). U confounds adherence even after randomisation.

The fact that ITT is called “conservative” (biased toward the null) tacitly acknowledges that PP is the true target — ITT is its lower bound, not the answer.

Intention-To-Treat
Why ITT is attractive in RCTs
  • Randomisation operates on treatment assignment. Z is randomised → no confounding. No covariate adjustment required.
  • Valid test of the null (placebo, double-blind). If the treatment has no effect, ITT correctly finds null regardless of adherence. A significant result guarantees a true effect at least as large.
  • Simple and attractive. No post-randomisation tracking, no time-varying modelling, fewer analyst degrees of freedom.
When ITT is not universally valid

1) ITT is not universally valid

  • When the randomised trial is not double-blinded.
  • When studying harms — bias toward null is anti-conservative for safety.
  • When informative censoring is present.

2) ITT estimates depend on adherence

The direction and magnitude of the ITT effect depends on the adherence pattern. If the comparator is not placebo, part of the effect is mediated by adherence only. Long follow-up is by design more prone to substantial loss-to-follow-up and non-adherence.

3) ITT does not provide the complete picture

May not be a valid measure of effectiveness. A true effectiveness measure should incorporate the effects of assignment awareness. For patients who are interested in the benefit-risk profile of the treatment when taken as instructed, ITT does not answer their question.

In non-inferiority trials with an active comparator, differential adherence can make an inferior treatment appear non-inferior — or vice versa. The direction of the ITT bias is unpredictable.

Per-Protocol
Classical view

Why PP is unattractive in RCTs

  • PP effect cannot be guaranteed by randomisation.
  • Generally requires adjustment for prognostic factors that predict adherence.
  • Requires untestable assumptions for identification and estimation.
Coronary Drug Project cautionary tale

Placebo-arm adherers had ~9 pp lower mortality than non-adherers — yet placebo cannot cause mortality. Unmeasured prognostic factors explained the gap. A 2015 reanalysis with g-methods reduced it to 2.5 pp. Proper causal adjustment is the solution, not abandoning PP.

Advantages

Why PP has decisive advantages

  • Mitigates ITT main problems — valid regardless of blinding; correct direction for safety outcomes.
  • Interpretation does not depend on trial-specific adherence. PP is comparable and transportable across studies.
  • More relevant for clinical decisions. Patients want the effect of the treatment when taken as instructed.
  • Adherence/effectiveness may be greater in RCTs due to earlier detection of problems — PP corrects for this rather than incorporating it.
How to estimate PP validly (Hernán & Robins 2017)
  • Do not censor participants who stop for clinical reasons.
  • Censor when adherence status becomes unknown, not when outcomes are known.
  • Adjust for time-varying confounders via g-methods (IPW, G-formula) — standard regression introduces feedback bias.
Conclusion
ITT and PP are equal under perfect conditions
ITT effect = PP effect
when all participants adhere fully throughout follow-up

As non-adherence grows, the two estimates diverge. The gap quantifies the cost of non-adherence in that context and is itself informative. Reporting both is the more complete output.

Conclusion

In RCT

Exclusive reliance on ITT effect estimates is hard to justify for randomised trials with:

  • Substantial non-adherence
  • Evaluating harms
  • Not placebo-controlled or not double-blinded
Relationships to the estimand framework ICH E9(R1)

ITT corresponds to the Treatment Policy Strategy; PP (sometimes referred to as “on-treatment”) aligns with the Hypothetical Strategy. Each intercurrent event requires a clearly defined strategy.

RWE studies

In RWE studies: treatment intention is unknown, assignment is non-randomised, awareness can lead to behavioural changes, adherence tends to be lower, loss-to-follow-up is often informative, and the comparator is rarely equivalent to a placebo. Therefore, prioritising PP analysis as the primary, complemented by ITT analysis, could be standard practice.
