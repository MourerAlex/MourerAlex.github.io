2025-03-15 Causal Inference

Why Model?

From data and question to estimation strategy.

A statistical model is an approximation: a set of parametric assumptions that allows patterns observed in the data to be extrapolated to the target population, filling gaps where direct observations are absent or too sparse. Without such structure, the raw information one can extract from data is often insufficient to answer the causal questions we care about.

Running scenario — used throughout this post

Two drugs are available in clinical practice to reduce the risk of heart failure in a susceptible population.

Drug A is taken weekly and is the more effective option against heart failure. It carries a known risk of blindness: approximately 2% of patients on Drug A develop blindness during treatment. This risk is not uniform — it doubles for women relative to men, and increases with age. Drug A is under active pharmacovigilance surveillance.

Drug B is less effective against heart failure but carries no blindness risk, making it the safer option from an adverse-event perspective.

Research question: What is the causal effect of Drug A compared with Drug B on heart failure risk in the population of eligible patients? This question arises because no direct evidence exists — existing trials compared Drug A against placebo, leaving the head-to-head comparison unanswered.

1. Three questions for every causal analysis

A useful framework for structuring any epidemiologic investigation asks three questions in sequence: What do I want? What do I have? What do I do? Ung and Dahabreh (2025) note that while many methodological frameworks emphasize the procedural steps of an analysis — the "what do I do" — the most rigorous ones begin by making the research question itself explicit, then account for what the available data can and cannot support, before selecting a method. These three questions define the overarching structure of any well-conducted study and naturally recur as each component of the design and analysis is worked out.

The order matters. Picking a method before stating what you want, or before understanding what you have, is one of the most common sources of confusion in applied causal inference. This post works through all three questions using the scenario above and, in doing so, arrives at the question that motivates the rest of the series: why do we need a model?

The estimand is fixed by the research question. Given the estimand and the data, the estimator produces the final estimate. Grey arrows reflect that the research question and available data inform each other (which variables to collect, what is feasible) — but this is secondary to the main estimation flow.

2. What do we have? The data

We have a large administrative database of patients initiating one of the two treatments. The data are clean — no missing values, no measurement error, no censoring. Each patient's record contains: treatment received ($A \in \{\text{Drug A},\, \text{Drug B}\}$), age (continuous, in years), sex (binary), and heart failure event during follow-up (binary $Y$).

Treatment was not randomly assigned. Physicians prescribed based on clinical judgment, and that judgment has a systematic pattern: because of Drug A's blindness risk, older patients and women are preferentially steered toward Drug B — precisely the patients most susceptible to that adverse event. Age and sex therefore simultaneously determine treatment choice and independently affect heart failure risk. This is confounding by indication.

A patient population that received Drug A will be systematically younger and more male than one that received Drug B, not because of any pharmacological difference between the drugs, but because of how prescribing decisions were made. Any crude comparison of outcomes conflates the causal effect of treatment with this baseline imbalance.

Figure 1 — Age and sex ($\mathbf{L}$) affect both treatment $A$ and outcome $Y$, opening a backdoor path $A \leftarrow \mathbf{L} \rightarrow Y$ that biases any unadjusted comparison between the two drugs.

3. What do we want? From research question to estimand

With the research question stated and the data described, the next step is to translate that question into a precise mathematical object: the estimand. This translation matters more than it might seem. Two analysts working from the same informal question can produce incompatible results if they are targeting different quantities — for instance, an effect in the treated population versus the full eligible population, or a short-term risk versus a longer horizon. Precision in the estimand is what makes an analysis interpretable and its results comparable across studies.

Once the estimand is fixed, it determines what follows: which identification assumptions are needed, which confounders must be adjusted for, and what a correctly specified estimator would look like. An estimate produced without a stated estimand is a number in search of a question. The chain below is the structure this series commits to:

Concept Definition In this analysis
Research question The scientific or clinical question, in plain language Does Drug A cause fewer heart failures than Drug B?
Estimand The precise mathematical quantity that answers the research question $\psi = \E[Y^{\text{A}}] - \E[Y^{\text{B}}]$
Estimator The procedure applied to data to compute an estimate of the estimand IPW, G-formula, doubly robust methods
Estimate The numerical result obtained from applying the estimator to the data The risk difference, with its uncertainty

The estimate is only the end product of this chain. It inherits its meaning from the estimand and its validity from the estimator. The order — question, estimand, estimator, estimate — is not a formality. It is the structure that makes the analysis interpretable and the conclusions defensible.

Formally, for each patient $i$ and each treatment $a$, the potential outcome $Y^a_i$ denotes the heart failure outcome that patient $i$ would experience under treatment $a$, possibly contrary to what actually happened. Because each patient receives only one treatment, only one potential outcome is ever observed: $Y^1_i$ if Drug A was prescribed, $Y^0_i$ if Drug B was prescribed (where 1 and 0 denote the two drugs for concision). The estimand is the average treatment effect (ATE):

$$\psi = \E[Y^{\text{A}}] - \E[Y^{\text{B}}]$$

This is the difference in population-mean heart failure risk under universal assignment to Drug A versus universal assignment to Drug B. We expect $\psi < 0$: Drug A is the more effective option, so the counterfactual risk under Drug A is lower. Having stated the estimand, we can now ask how to estimate it from the available data — and whether the raw data are sufficient to do so.

Scope of this post

We work here with clean data — no missing values, no censoring, no measurement error. Each of these complications introduces additional identifiability challenges and will be addressed in dedicated posts. Setting them aside isolates the core problem: estimating a causal effect from observational data in the presence of measured confounding.

4. What to do? From stratification to modeling

Under conditional exchangeability, positivity, and consistency (the identifiability conditions detailed in Module 0 Assumptions), $\psi$ is identified by the standardized risk difference:

$$\psi \;=\; \sum_{\ell}\, \E[Y \mid A = \text{A},\, \mathbf{L} = \ell]\;\P(\mathbf{L} = \ell) \;-\; \sum_{\ell}\, \E[Y \mid A = \text{B},\, \mathbf{L} = \ell]\;\P(\mathbf{L} = \ell)$$

The recipe: estimate the outcome risk under each treatment within every stratum $\ell$ of the confounders, then average over the marginal distribution of $\mathbf{L}$. This is standardization — the nonparametric approach Hernán & Robins develop in Chapter 2 (2020). When the confounder vector is discrete and the strata are well-populated, it is unbiased and requires no modeling assumptions. With a binary confounder like sex (two strata), it is entirely tractable.

4.1 When nonparametric approaches break down

Age complicates this immediately. Being continuous, it assigns each individual to their own stratum in principle: no two patients need share an identical age value, and estimating a risk among patients with exactly age $t = 47.3$ years is meaningless when no one else in the dataset has that value. The natural response is categorization — grouping age into, say, five bins. This restores feasibility at the cost of residual confounding within each bin.

Categorization works for a single continuous variable. The deeper problem surfaces as the confounder vector grows. Physicians weighing Drug A's blindness risk likely consider comorbidities beyond age and sex: hypertension, diabetes, prior cardiac events, renal function, smoking status. Five such binary confounders, combined with five age categories and sex, already produce:

$$5_{\,\text{age}} \;\times\; 2_{\,\text{sex}} \;\times\; 2^5_{\,\text{binary}} \;=\; 320 \text{ strata} \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; 640 \text{ cells to fill}$$

A substantial fraction of those 640 cells will be empty or near-empty in any real database. The nonparametric estimator, which asks the data within each cell to speak for themselves, becomes undefined or wildly imprecise. This is the curse of dimensionality: the very act of conditioning on all measured confounders — which identification requires — exhausts the data's ability to say anything informative (Hernán & Robins, Ch. 10.5; Robins and Ritov, 1997). Each additional binary confounder doubles the number of strata, and the problem worsens exponentially.

4.2 The solution: a model

The way out is to supplement the data with a model: an a priori restriction on how the outcome or treatment probabilities vary with covariates. Hernán & Robins (Ch. 11) define a model precisely as such a restriction — it adds information not in the data, which is exactly what is needed when the data are too sparse to estimate stratum-specific risks independently. A logistic regression, for instance, assumes the log-odds of the outcome is a linear function of the covariates:

$$\log\frac{\P(Y=1 \mid A,\, \mathbf{L})}{1 - \P(Y=1 \mid A,\, \mathbf{L})} \;=\; \theta_0 + \theta_1 A + \theta_2\,\text{Age} + \theta_3\,\text{Sex} + \cdots$$

Rather than estimating 640 cell-specific risks independently, this model fits a single smooth surface over the covariate space using all observations jointly. Patients in sparse or empty strata receive predicted risks informed by neighboring strata through the model's parametric structure. The restriction may not hold exactly — it almost never does — but it makes estimation tractable where the nonparametric estimator collapses. The cost is potential bias from misspecification; the benefit is finite-sample feasibility.

This is why we model: not out of a belief that parametric forms are true, but because any realistic confounder space — even a modest one — leaves the data unable to speak for themselves. Concretely, modeling will allow us to estimate either the propensity score $\P(A = \text{A} \mid \mathbf{L})$, the outcome regression $\E[Y \mid A, \mathbf{L}]$, or both, and to plug these into the standardization formula to recover a tractable estimate of $\psi$. Those are the building blocks of IPW, G-methods, and doubly robust estimators covered in this series.

Looking ahead

A statistical model that smooths the data is not yet a causal model. It can borrow information across strata and produce predictions, but it does not by itself tell us which variables to condition on, which adjustments open new biases rather than closing old ones, or how the data-generating process relates to the counterfactual world. That is the subject for later.

Why causal model?

A statistical model becomes a causal model once its parameters are anchored to a well-defined estimand through explicit identification assumptions — exchangeability, positivity, and consistency. These assumptions license the G-formula: a generalization of standardization that recovers mean potential outcomes as a functional of the observed data distribution. In practice, fitting parametric models for the required conditional distributions — the parametric G-formula — extends this identification strategy from a static snapshot of baseline confounders to full sequences of time-varying exposures and covariate histories. The extension is not merely a technical generalization: in a longitudinal setting, classical regression conditions on post-treatment covariates that either lie on the causal pathway or have been affected by prior treatment, blocking or distorting the causal effect of interest; the same mechanism operates under informative censoring, where standard survival models condition on variables that jointly predict dropout and the outcome, introducing selection bias. When earlier treatment values affect later confounders, which in turn affect subsequent treatment — confounder feedback — no regression-based estimator can remove the resulting bias without explicitly targeting a causal parameter through G-methods or marginal structural models.

Even in the comparatively simple setting of baseline-only confounding with non-informative censoring, the causal framework delivers three further advantages: doubly robust estimators combine treatment and outcome models so that consistency of only one is sufficient, removing the need to choose between a propensity-score analysis and an outcome regression at the outset; extending those components with machine learning — as in targeted maximum likelihood estimation (TMLE) — relaxes parametric functional-form assumptions and protects against the misspecification that any fixed parametric family is prone to; and anchoring the analysis to a pre-specified target parameter keeps the focus on the quantity of scientific interest rather than on a practitioner’s preferred estimator, such as the Cox proportional hazards ratio, whose causal interpretation depends on assumptions that are rarely stated explicitly.
