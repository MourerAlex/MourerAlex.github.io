2. The new data structure

Within each time interval $k > 0$, the paper assumes a temporal order:

$$(C_k, \quad D_k, \quad Y_k, \quad L_{D,k}, \quad L_{Y,k})$$

$L_{D,k}$ is realised before $L_{Y,k}$. This is a modelling choice, not a property of the data. In DES: blood pressure ($L_{D,k}$, affected by the coagulation component, predicts cardiovascular death) is measured before PSA ($L_{Y,k}$, affected by the testosterone component, predicts prostate cancer).

The partition $L_k = (L_{Y,k},\, L_{D,k})$ is also a modelling choice. The investigator decides which covariates belong to the $D$-pathway and which to the $Y$-pathway. This choice determines which level of the isolation hierarchy is assumed (Section 8).

3. The four dismissible conditions

The 2020 paper had two dismissible conditions. Written as hazard equalities, they said:

$\Delta 1$: $\P(Y^{\color{teal}{a_Y},\, \color{orange}{a_D}=1}_{k+1} = 1 \mid \ldots) = \P(Y^{\color{teal}{a_Y},\, \color{orange}{a_D}=0}_{k+1} = 1 \mid \ldots)$ — the $Y$ hazard does not depend on $\color{orange}{a_D}$.

$\Delta 2$: $\P(D^{\color{teal}{a_Y}=1,\, \color{orange}{a_D}}_{k+1} = 1 \mid \ldots) = \P(D^{\color{teal}{a_Y}=0,\, \color{orange}{a_D}}_{k+1} = 1 \mid \ldots)$ — the $D$ hazard does not depend on $\color{teal}{a_Y}$.

The 2021 paper restates these as conditional independencies and adds two more for the covariate densities. The notation changes but the content of $\Delta 1$ and $\Delta 2$ is the same.

The trial $G$. In the real 2-arm trial, DES is one pill: everyone who gets it receives both testosterone suppression and coagulation ($A_Y = A_D = 1$), and everyone on placebo receives neither ($A_Y = A_D = 0$). The combinations $(A_Y = 1, A_D = 0)$ and $(A_Y = 0, A_D = 1)$ never happen. Trial $G$ is the imagined 4-arm trial where $A_Y$ and $A_D$ are separately and independently randomised — all four combinations exist. The $(G)$ label on each variable (e.g., $Y_{k+1}(G)$) is bookkeeping: it says "this quantity belongs to the imagined trial, not the real data." The $(G)$ disappears when the proof shows only 2-arm data is needed.

3.1 — Condition (34): Y hazard

$$Y^{\bar{c}=0}_{k+1}(G) \;\ind\; A_D(G) \;\mid\; A_Y(G),\; D^{\bar{c}=0}_{k+1}(G) = Y^{\bar{c}=0}_k(G) = 0,\; \bar{L}^{\bar{c}=0}_k(G)$$

The cause-specific hazard of $Y$ among event-free individuals does not depend on $\color{orange}{a_D}$, conditional on the full covariate history $\bar{L}_k$. Same as the 2020 condition, now conditioning on time-varying covariates instead of baseline only.

Full explanation (same logic as Part 4a Section 3.6)

In DES: among individuals who are alive and prostate-cancer-free at time $k$, with the same covariate history (blood pressure, PSA, etc.), the risk of prostate cancer at $k+1$ does not depend on whether the coagulation component was active ($\color{orange}{a_D} = 1$) or not ($\color{orange}{a_D} = 0$). The coagulation effect of DES does not change the prostate cancer hazard. This is a biological claim, not testable in a two-arm trial.

3.2 — Condition (35): D hazard

$$D^{\bar{c}=0}_{k+1}(G) \;\ind\; A_Y(G) \;\mid\; A_D(G),\; D^{\bar{c}=0}_k(G) = Y^{\bar{c}=0}_k(G) = 0,\; \bar{L}^{\bar{c}=0}_k(G)$$

The cause-specific hazard of $D$ among event-free individuals does not depend on $\color{teal}{a_Y}$. The testosterone suppression of DES does not change the cardiovascular death hazard. Same logic as (34) with roles swapped.

3.3 — Condition (36): $L_Y$ density — new

$$L^{\bar{c}=0}_{Y,k}(G) \;\ind\; A_D(G) \;\mid\; A_Y(G),\; Y^{\bar{c}=0}_k(G) = D^{\bar{c}=0}_k(G) = 0,\; \bar{L}^{\bar{c}=0}_{k-1}(G),\; L^{\bar{c}=0}_{D,k}(G)$$

The density of $L_{Y,k}$ among event-free individuals does not depend on $\color{orange}{a_D}$, given $\color{teal}{a_Y}$, the past covariate history, and $L_{D,k}$ at the same time. In DES: PSA level at time $k$ does not depend on whether the coagulation component was active, once testosterone suppression status, the past, and current blood pressure are known. The coagulation pathway does not affect PSA.

$L_{D,k}$ appears in the conditioning because it is realised before $L_{Y,k}$ in the temporal order — it is already known when $L_{Y,k}$ is determined.

3.4 — Condition (37): $L_D$ density — new

$$L^{\bar{c}=0}_{D,k}(G) \;\ind\; A_Y(G) \;\mid\; A_D(G),\; D^{\bar{c}=0}_k(G) = Y^{\bar{c}=0}_k(G) = 0,\; \bar{L}^{\bar{c}=0}_{k-1}(G)$$

The density of $L_{D,k}$ among event-free individuals does not depend on $\color{teal}{a_Y}$, given $\color{orange}{a_D}$ and the past covariate history. In DES: blood pressure at time $k$ does not depend on whether the testosterone suppression component was active, once coagulation status and the past are known. The testosterone pathway does not affect blood pressure.

Note: $L_{Y,k}$ does not appear in the conditioning — unlike condition (36), which conditions on $L_{D,k}$. This asymmetry reflects the temporal order: $L_{D,k}$ is realised before $L_{Y,k}$, so at the moment $L_{D,k}$ is determined, $L_{Y,k}$ does not yet exist. All past $L_Y$ values (times $1, \ldots, k-1$) are in the conditioning through $\bar{L}_{k-1}$. Only the current $L_{Y,k}$ is excluded.

4. The generalized g-formula

The derivation follows the same structure as Part 4a Section 4: law of total probability → partition over first-event time → chain rule → dismissible conditions separate the components → decomposition collapses to single-world → exchangeability and consistency convert to observed data. The chain rule now produces five terms per time step instead of three, and the four dismissible conditions separate all five. The iterative peeling (Part 4a Section 8) now converts four counterfactuals per round ($Y_{k+1}$, $D_{k+1}$, $L_{Y,k+1}$, $L_{D,k+1}$) instead of two.

Full derivation from the target (click to expand)

Step 1 — Target:

$$\P(Y^{a_Y,\, a_D,\, \bar{c}=0}_{K+1} = 1)$$

The risk of $Y$ by $K+1$ had $A_Y$ been set to $a_Y$, $A_D$ been set to $a_D$, and nobody been censored.

Step 2 — Law of total probability over $\bar{L}_K$: standardise over all possible covariate histories. Unlike 2020, the sum is over the full history $\bar{l}_K$, not just baseline $l_0$:

$$= \sum_{\bar{l}_K} \P(Y^{a_Y,\, a_D,\, \bar{c}=0}_{K+1} = 1 \mid \bar{L}^{a_Y,\, a_D,\, \bar{c}=0}_K = \bar{l}_K)\; \P(\bar{L}^{a_Y,\, a_D,\, \bar{c}=0}_K = \bar{l}_K)$$

By the definition of total probability, $\P(B) = \sum_{\bar{l}} \P(B \mid \bar{L} = \bar{l})\P(\bar{L} = \bar{l})$. This sums over every possible trajectory of covariates through time.

Step 3 — Partition over first-event time: if $Y$ occurs by $K+1$, it occurs at some specific first time $s+1$. Sum over all possibilities:

$$= \sum_{\bar{l}_K} \sum_{s=0}^{K} \P(Y^{a_Y,a_D,\bar{c}=0}_{s+1} = 1,\; D^{a_Y,a_D,\bar{c}=0}_{s+1} = 0,\; Y^{a_Y,a_D,\bar{c}=0}_s = 0 \mid \bar{L}^{a_Y,a_D,\bar{c}=0}_s = \bar{l}_s)\; \P(\bar{L}^{a_Y,a_D,\bar{c}=0}_K = \bar{l}_K)$$

Step 4 — Chain rule: factor into five components per time step. At each time $j$, peel off: event-free from $D$ at $j+1$, event-free from $Y$ at $j$, the density of $L_{Y,j}$, the density of $L_{D,j}$. At time $s+1$, the hazard of $Y$. All carry two-world counterfactual superscripts $(a_Y, a_D)$.

Step 5 — Apply the four dismissible conditions:

Condition (34): the $Y$ hazard does not depend on $\color{orange}{a_D}$. In the hazard term $\P(Y^{a_Y, a_D, \bar{c}=0}_{s+1} = 1 \mid \ldots)$, replace $\color{orange}{a_D}$ with $\color{teal}{a_Y}$. Same for the $Y$ event-free terms.

Condition (35): the $D$ event-free terms do not depend on $\color{teal}{a_Y}$. Replace $\color{teal}{a_Y}$ with $\color{orange}{a_D}$.

Condition (36): $f(L_{Y,k})$ does not depend on $\color{orange}{a_D}$. Replace $\color{orange}{a_D}$ with $\color{teal}{a_Y}$.

Condition (37): $f(L_{D,k})$ does not depend on $\color{teal}{a_Y}$. Replace $\color{teal}{a_Y}$ with $\color{orange}{a_D}$.

Step 6 — Decomposition: every term now has both components set to the same value.

$Y$ terms, $Y$ event-free, $f(L_Y)$: $a_Y$ and $a_D = a_Y$ → $Y^{a = a_Y}$. Single-world under $a = \color{teal}{a_Y}$.

$D$ event-free, $f(L_D)$: $a_Y = a_D$ and $a_D$ → $D^{a = a_D}$. Single-world under $a = \color{orange}{a_D}$.

Step 7 — Exchangeability and consistency (iterative peeling):

Same ratio trick as Part 4a Section 8. Write each counterfactual conditional probability as a ratio with observed baseline in the conditioning. Then iterate from $k = 0$:

At each round $k$:

Sub-step 1: E2 at $k$ (now conditioning on $\bar{L}_k$) — add $C_{k+1} = 0$.

Sub-step 2: Consistency at $k+1$ — convert $Y^{a,\bar{c}=0}_{k+1} = Y_{k+1}$, $D^{a,\bar{c}=0}_{k+1} = D_{k+1}$, $L^{a,\bar{c}=0}_{Y,k+1} = L_{Y,k+1}$, $L^{a,\bar{c}=0}_{D,k+1} = L_{D,k+1}$. Four conversions per round instead of two.

Sub-step 3: Move observed values into conditioning. Conditioning now observed through $k+1$.

Tracking table:

After roundCounterfactuals remainingObserved conditioning through
Ratio$Y, D, L_Y, L_D$ at times $1 \ldots s+1$time $0$
$k = 0$$Y, D, L_Y, L_D$ at times $2 \ldots s+1$time $1$
$\vdots$$\vdots$$\vdots$
$k = s$$\emptyset$time $s+1$

After all rounds, reconstitute as observed-data conditional probabilities. Each $Y$ term at $A = \color{teal}{a_Y}$, each $D$ and $L_D$ term at $A = \color{orange}{a_D}$, each $L_Y$ term at $A = \color{teal}{a_Y}$.

The result — the generalized g-formula:

$$\P(Y^{a_Y,\, a_D,\, \bar{c}=0}_{K+1} = 1) = \sum_{\bar{l}_K} \sum_{s=0}^{K} \underbrace{\P(Y_{s+1} = 1 \mid C_{s+1} = D_{s+1} = Y_s = 0,\; \bar{L}_s = \bar{l}_s,\; A = \color{teal}{a_Y})}_{\text{Block A: CS hazard of } Y \text{ from } \color{teal}{a_Y} \text{ arm}}$$ $$\times \prod_{j=0}^{s} \Big\{ \underbrace{\P(D_{j+1} = 0 \mid C_{j+1} = D_j = Y_j = 0,\; \bar{L}_j = \bar{l}_j,\; A = \color{orange}{a_D})}_{\text{Block B: event-free from } D \text{ from } \color{orange}{a_D} \text{ arm}}$$ $$\times \underbrace{\P(Y_j = 0 \mid C_j = D_j = Y_{j-1} = 0,\; \bar{L}_{j-1} = \bar{l}_{j-1},\; A = \color{teal}{a_Y})}_{\text{Block C: event-free from } Y \text{ from } \color{teal}{a_Y} \text{ arm}}$$ $$\times \underbrace{f(L_{Y,j} = l_{Y,j} \mid C_j = Y_j = D_j = 0,\; \bar{L}_{j-1} = \bar{l}_{j-1},\; L_{D,j} = l_{D,j},\; A = \color{teal}{a_Y})}_{\text{Block D: density of } L_Y \text{ from } \color{teal}{a_Y} \text{ arm}}$$ $$\times \underbrace{f(L_{D,j} = l_{D,j} \mid C_j = Y_j = D_j = 0,\; \bar{L}_{j-1} = \bar{l}_{j-1},\; A = \color{orange}{a_D})}_{\text{Block E: density of } L_D \text{ from } \color{orange}{a_D} \text{ arm}} \Big\}$$

Blocks A, C, D come from the $A = \color{teal}{a_Y}$ arm. Blocks B, E come from the $A = \color{orange}{a_D}$ arm. Block D conditions on $L_{D,j}$ (temporal order: $L_D$ before $L_Y$). Block E does not condition on $L_{Y,j}$ (not yet realised).

When $\bar{L}_k = L_0$ for all $k$, Blocks D and E do not depend on $A$ (Lemma 8 of Appendix C) and the sum collapses to baseline only — recovering the 2020 g-formula from Part 4a.

5. From the g-formula to IPW Representation 1: the new weight $W_{L_D}$

5.1 — What the $\color{teal}{a_Y}$ arm gives

Standing in the $A = \color{teal}{a_Y}$ arm, Blocks A ($Y$ hazard), C ($Y$ event-free), and D ($f(L_Y)$) are already at the correct value $\color{teal}{a_Y}$. But Block B ($D$ event-free) and Block E ($f(L_D)$) are at $\color{teal}{a_Y}$ instead of $\color{orange}{a_D}$. Two things need fixing instead of one.

5.2 — $W_D$ swaps the D event-free

Same multiply-and-divide trick as Part 4a Section 5.3:

$$W_{D,s}(a_Y, a_D) = \prod_{j=0}^{s} \frac{\P(D_{j+1} = 0 \mid C_{j+1} = D_j = Y_j = 0,\; \bar{L}_j,\; A = \color{orange}{a_D})}{\P(D_{j+1} = 0 \mid C_{j+1} = D_j = Y_j = 0,\; \bar{L}_j,\; A = \color{teal}{a_Y})}$$

The denominator cancels the $D$ event-free distribution present in the $\color{teal}{a_Y}$ arm. The numerator inserts the one from the $\color{orange}{a_D}$ arm.

Full derivation of the multiply-and-divide step (same as Part 4a Section 5.3–5.4)

The $D$ event-free term at $\color{orange}{a_D}$ can be written as the $D$ event-free term at $\color{teal}{a_Y}$ times a correction factor:

$$\P(D_{j+1} = 0 \mid \ldots, A = \color{orange}{a_D}) = \P(D_{j+1} = 0 \mid \ldots, A = \color{teal}{a_Y}) \times \frac{\P(D_{j+1} = 0 \mid \ldots, A = \color{orange}{a_D})}{\P(D_{j+1} = 0 \mid \ldots, A = \color{teal}{a_Y})}$$

This is multiplying and dividing by the same quantity — pure algebra, no assumptions used. Substitute into the g-formula. The $\P(D_{j+1} = 0 \mid \ldots, A = \color{teal}{a_Y})$ factor now matches what is observed in the $\color{teal}{a_Y}$ arm. Collecting the ratios into a product gives $W_{D,s}$. After substitution, everything except $W_{D,s}$ and the density terms is the joint probability of "event-free through $s$, $Y$ at $s+1$" in the $\color{teal}{a_Y}$ arm.

5.3 — $W_{L_D}$ swaps the $L_D$ density — the problem

Block E needs $f(L_{D,j})$ at $A = \color{orange}{a_D}$. In the $\color{teal}{a_Y}$ arm, it is at $A = \color{teal}{a_Y}$. The same multiply-and-divide trick gives:

$$W_{L_D, s} = \prod_{j=0}^{s} \frac{f(L_{D,j} = l_{D,j} \mid C_j = Y_j = D_j = 0,\; \bar{L}_{j-1} = \bar{l}_{j-1},\; A = \color{orange}{a_D})}{f(L_{D,j} = l_{D,j} \mid C_j = Y_j = D_j = 0,\; \bar{L}_{j-1} = \bar{l}_{j-1},\; A = \color{teal}{a_Y})}$$

This is a ratio of conditional densities. If $L_{D,k}$ is continuous (e.g., blood pressure), computing this requires a model for the conditional distribution of blood pressure given history and treatment — much harder than a logistic regression.

5.4 — The Bayes’ rule trick: densities to propensity scores

Bayes’ rule says: for any variables $X$, $A$, $B$:

$$f(X \mid A = a, B) = \frac{\P(A = a \mid X, B) \;\cdot\; f(X \mid B)}{\P(A = a \mid B)}$$

This is the definition of conditional probability rearranged. $f(X \mid B)$ is the density of $X$ given $B$ but not given $A$. $\P(A = a \mid X, B)$ is the probability of treatment given $X$ and $B$ — a propensity score. $\P(A = a \mid B)$ is the probability of treatment given $B$ alone.

Apply this to the numerator of one factor of $W_{L_D}$. Let $X = L_{D,j}$, $B = (C_j = Y_j = D_j = 0, \bar{L}_{j-1})$:

$$f(L_{D,j} \mid \ldots,\; A = \color{orange}{a_D}) = \frac{\P(A = \color{orange}{a_D} \mid L_{D,j},\; \bar{L}_{j-1},\; \ldots) \;\cdot\; f(L_{D,j} \mid \bar{L}_{j-1},\; \ldots)}{\P(A = \color{orange}{a_D} \mid \bar{L}_{j-1},\; \ldots)}$$

Apply the same to the denominator:

$$f(L_{D,j} \mid \ldots,\; A = \color{teal}{a_Y}) = \frac{\P(A = \color{teal}{a_Y} \mid L_{D,j},\; \bar{L}_{j-1},\; \ldots) \;\cdot\; f(L_{D,j} \mid \bar{L}_{j-1},\; \ldots)}{\P(A = \color{teal}{a_Y} \mid \bar{L}_{j-1},\; \ldots)}$$

Take the ratio numerator / denominator. The $f(L_{D,j} \mid \bar{L}_{j-1}, \ldots)$ terms appear in both — they cancel. This is the density we did not want to model. What remains:

$$\frac{f(L_{D,j} \mid \ldots, A = \color{orange}{a_D})}{f(L_{D,j} \mid \ldots, A = \color{teal}{a_Y})} = \underbrace{\frac{\P(A = \color{orange}{a_D} \mid C_j = Y_j = D_j = 0,\; L_{D,j},\; \bar{L}_{j-1})}{\P(A = \color{teal}{a_Y} \mid C_j = Y_j = D_j = 0,\; L_{D,j},\; \bar{L}_{j-1})}}_{\text{propensity score ratio \#1: includes } L_{D,j}} \times \underbrace{\frac{\P(A = \color{teal}{a_Y} \mid C_j = Y_j = D_j = 0,\; \bar{L}_{j-1})}{\P(A = \color{orange}{a_D} \mid C_j = Y_j = D_j = 0,\; \bar{L}_{j-1})}}_{\text{propensity score ratio \#2: excludes } L_{D,j}}$$

The density ratio has been replaced by a product of two propensity score ratios — the probability of treatment given covariates. This is the point of the Bayes’ rule trick: modelling a conditional density (the distribution of blood pressure given history and treatment) is hard, especially when $L_{D,k}$ is continuous. Modelling a propensity score (the probability of treatment given blood pressure and history) is a standard logistic regression. The trick trades one hard problem for two easy ones. Part 5 details the implementation.

5.5 — Full IPW Representation 1

$$\nu_{a_Y, a_D, k} = \sum_{s=0}^{k} \E\Big[W_{C,s}(a_Y) \cdot W_{D,s}(a_Y, a_D) \cdot W_{L_D, s}(\hat{\beta}_{L1}, \hat{\beta}_{L2}) \cdot Y_{s+1}(1 - Y_s)(1 - D_{s+1}) \;\Big|\; A = \color{teal}{a_Y}\Big]$$

Three weights: $W_C$ removes censoring. $W_D$ swaps the $D$ distribution from $\color{teal}{a_Y}$ to $\color{orange}{a_D}$. $W_{L_D}$ swaps the $L_D$ density from $\color{teal}{a_Y}$ to $\color{orange}{a_D}$. What remains is the g-formula.

6. IPW Representation 2: the mirror with $W_{L_Y}$

Stand in the $A = \color{orange}{a_D}$ arm. Blocks B ($D$ event-free) and E ($f(L_D)$) are correct. Blocks A ($Y$ hazard), C ($Y$ event-free), and D ($f(L_Y)$) are wrong — at $\color{orange}{a_D}$ instead of $\color{teal}{a_Y}$.

$W_Y$ swaps the $Y$ hazard and $Y$ event-free.

$W_Y$ formula (same structure as Part 4a Section 6)
$$W_{Y,s}(a_D, a_Y) = \frac{\P(Y_{s+1} = 1 \mid D_{s+1} = Y_s = \bar{C}_{s+1} = 0,\; \bar{L}_s,\; A = \color{teal}{a_Y})}{\P(Y_{s+1} = 1 \mid D_{s+1} = Y_s = \bar{C}_{s+1} = 0,\; \bar{L}_s,\; A = \color{orange}{a_D})} \times \prod_{j=0}^{s-1} \frac{\P(Y_{j+1} = 0 \mid D_{j+1} = Y_j = \bar{C}_{j+1} = 0,\; \bar{L}_j,\; A = \color{teal}{a_Y})}{\P(Y_{j+1} = 0 \mid D_{j+1} = Y_j = \bar{C}_{j+1} = 0,\; \bar{L}_j,\; A = \color{orange}{a_D})}$$

The first ratio swaps the $Y$ hazard at $s+1$. The second product swaps the $Y$ event-free at every earlier time $j$. Both are needed because $Y$ appears in two places in the g-formula: once as the hazard (probability of $Y = 1$) and once as the event-free factor (probability of $Y = 0$). These are different quantities — $\P(Y = 1)/\P(Y = 1)$ is not the same as $\P(Y = 0)/\P(Y = 0)$ — so they cannot be combined into a single uniform product.

$W_{L_Y}$ swaps $f(L_Y)$ using the same Bayes’ rule trick, but the conditioning sets differ because of the temporal order:

$$W_{L_Y, s} = \prod_{j=0}^{s} \underbrace{\frac{\P(A = \color{teal}{a_Y} \mid C_j = Y_j = D_j = 0,\; \bar{L}_j)}{\P(A = \color{orange}{a_D} \mid C_j = Y_j = D_j = 0,\; \bar{L}_j)}}_{\text{ratio \#1: includes } L_{Y,j} \text{ (via } \bar{L}_j\text{)}} \times \underbrace{\frac{\P(A = \color{orange}{a_D} \mid C_j = Y_j = D_j = 0,\; L_{D,j},\; \bar{L}_{j-1})}{\P(A = \color{teal}{a_Y} \mid C_j = Y_j = D_j = 0,\; L_{D,j},\; \bar{L}_{j-1})}}_{\text{ratio \#2: excludes } L_{Y,j}}$$

The asymmetry with $W_{L_D}$: $W_{L_D}$’s ratios condition on $(L_{D,j}, \bar{L}_{j-1})$ and $\bar{L}_{j-1}$. $W_{L_Y}$’s ratios condition on $\bar{L}_j$ and $(L_{D,j}, \bar{L}_{j-1})$. This reflects what each weight swaps. $W_{L_D}$ swaps the density of $L_{D,j}$, so it needs propensity scores with and without $L_{D,j}$. $W_{L_Y}$ swaps the density of $L_{Y,j}$, so it needs propensity scores with and without $L_{Y,j}$. Since $L_{Y,j}$ comes after $L_{D,j}$ in the temporal order, "with $L_{Y,j}$" means conditioning on $\bar{L}_j$ (the full vector including $L_{Y,j}$), and "without $L_{Y,j}$" means conditioning on $(L_{D,j}, \bar{L}_{j-1})$.

The full IPW Representation 2:

$$\nu_{a_Y, a_D, k} = \sum_{s=0}^{k} \E\Big[W_{C,s}(a_D) \cdot W_{Y,s}(a_D, a_Y) \cdot W_{L_Y, s}(\hat{\beta}_{L3}, \hat{\beta}_{L4}) \cdot Y_{s+1}(1 - Y_s)(1 - D_{s+1}) \;\Big|\; A = \color{orange}{a_D}\Big]$$

Only the $Y$ hazard and the censoring probability need to be modelled. The $D$ hazard is observed directly in the $\color{orange}{a_D}$ arm.

When $L_{D,k} = \emptyset$ for all $k$ ($A_D$ partial isolation): $W_{L_D} = 1$, propensity models not needed, IPW Rep 1 reduces to the 2020 version. When $L_{Y,k} = \emptyset$: $W_{L_Y} = 1$, IPW Rep 2 reduces to the 2020 version. When both hold: full isolation, 2020 formula suffices entirely.

8. The isolation hierarchy and the partition

The choice of partition $L_k = (L_{Y,k},\, L_{D,k})$ determines which level of the isolation hierarchy is assumed. Appendix C of the 2021 paper formalises the connections:

Partition choiceIsolation levelPractical consequence
$L_{Y,k} = \emptyset$, $L_{D,k} = L_k$$A_Y$ partial isolation$W_{L_D} = 1$, skip propensity models for Rep 1
$L_{D,k} = \emptyset$, $L_{Y,k} = L_k$$A_D$ partial isolation$W_{L_Y} = 1$, skip propensity models for Rep 2
Both partitions workFull isolation$L_k \ind A$ given past; 2020 formula suffices
$Z_k$ partition failsNoneAt least one condition fails for any partition

$Z_k$ partition fails when at least one of three things holds: $A_Y$ directly causes $D_{k+1}$; $A_D$ directly causes $Y_{k+1}$; or both $A_Y$ and $A_D$ have direct arrows into the same covariate $W$ (the "mixed paint" problem). When any of these holds, no choice of measured covariates and no partition can rescue the dismissible conditions.

The IPW weights provide a data-driven check of the isolation level: if $W_{L_D} \approx 1$ for everyone, $A_D$ partial isolation is consistent with the data. If $W_{L_Y} \approx 1$, $A_Y$ partial isolation is consistent. If both are near 1, full isolation is plausible and the 2020 formula suffices.

9. Connection to path-specific effects

(To be developed.) The g-formula is algebraically identical to formulas for path-specific effects (Shpitser 2013; Robins, Richardson & Shpitser 2020). The difference is interpretive: separable effects are interventions on modified treatments, testable in a future 4-arm trial; path-specific effects are cross-world decompositions that no experiment can observe.
