2025-03-18 Causal Inference

Inverse Probability Weighting

When you can't randomize, reweight.

1. The Problem

Running Example

A public health agency wants to know whether a preventive drug reduces the risk of a disease (binary: yes/no). They have observational data from a large cohort. Two variables were measured at baseline: age (continuous, in years) and sex (binary: male/female). No randomized trial is available.

In an ideal world, we would conduct a randomized experiment: flip a coin for each person, give the drug to those who get heads, and compare disease rates between the two groups. Randomization ensures that, on average, the treated and untreated groups are comparable in every way—measured or unmeasured. Any difference in outcomes can be attributed to the drug.

But this is observational data. Physicians prescribed the drug based on clinical judgment. It turns out that older patients and male patients were more likely to receive the drug, because physicians perceived them as higher risk. The problem is that age and sex also independently affect disease risk. This creates confounding: the very factors that influence who gets treated also influence who gets sick.

Let's see what happens when we ignore this. Suppose we simply compare disease rates between treated and untreated individuals:

Group N Disease cases Risk
Treated ($A = 1$) 4,200 1,092 26.0%
Untreated ($A = 0$) 5,800 1,102 19.0%

Table 1.1 — Crude (unadjusted) comparison of disease risk by treatment status.

The naive risk difference is $26.0\% - 19.0\% = +7.0$ percentage points. The drug appears to increase disease risk. Should we conclude the drug is harmful?

The trap

No. The treated group is older and more male on average. These patients would have had higher disease risk regardless of treatment. The crude comparison confounds the effect of the drug with the effect of age and sex. We are comparing apples to oranges.

To see this concretely, imagine stratifying by age and sex. Within each stratum (e.g., 60-year-old males), the drug actually reduces disease risk. But because the drug was preferentially given to those at highest baseline risk, the overall comparison is reversed. This is a form of Simpson's paradox, and it is the fundamental challenge of causal inference from observational data.

The question becomes: how do we remove the distortion introduced by confounding, so that we can estimate what would have happened if everyone had been treated versus if everyone had been untreated?

2. What You Already Know

If you have taken an introductory epidemiology or biostatistics course, you likely already know at least two ways to handle confounding.

Stratification. Split the data into strata defined by the confounders (e.g., age groups $\times$ sex), estimate the treatment effect within each stratum, then combine using a weighted average (as in Mantel-Haenszel estimation). This works well when confounders are few and categorical. But with continuous confounders like age, or with many confounders, the strata become sparse and the approach breaks down.

Regression adjustment. Fit a model for the outcome—for instance, a logistic regression $\text{logit}\, \P(Y=1 \mid A, L) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 A + \beta_2 \text{Age} + \beta_3 \text{Sex}$—and read off the coefficient $\beta_1$ as the adjusted treatment effect. This is the workhorse of applied epidemiology. But it comes with a cost: the estimate depends entirely on getting the outcome model right. If the true relationship between confounders and outcome is nonlinear, or involves interactions you didn't include, the estimate will be biased. And you will typically have no way of knowing.

Both approaches share a limitation: they model the outcome. The validity of the causal estimate depends on correctly specifying how confounders relate to the outcome.

A different strategy

What if, instead of modeling the outcome, we modeled the treatment assignment? That is, instead of asking "how does disease risk depend on age, sex, and treatment?", we ask "how does the probability of being treated depend on age and sex?" This is the core idea behind inverse probability weighting.

3. The Idea

3.1 From randomization to reweighting

Let us return to the ideal experiment. In a randomized trial with a fair coin, each person has a 50% probability of receiving treatment, regardless of their age or sex. This means that the treated group and the untreated group are, on average, identical in every baseline characteristic. The comparison is fair.

In our observational study, the probability of treatment depends on the confounders. A 70-year-old male might have an 80% chance of receiving the drug, while a 30-year-old female might have only a 20% chance. Treatment groups are no longer comparable.

The insight of inverse probability weighting is this: if we know why people were treated—i.e., the probability of treatment given their characteristics—we can mathematically undo the selection process. We do this by giving each person a weight that is inversely proportional to the probability of the treatment they actually received.

3.2 The propensity score

Definition

The propensity score is the conditional probability of receiving treatment given the observed confounders: $$e(\mathbf{L}) = \P(A = 1 \mid \mathbf{L})$$ where $\mathbf{L} = (\text{Age}, \text{Sex})$ in our example. It was formalized by Rosenbaum and Rubin (1983).

In our running example, we might estimate this using logistic regression: $$\text{logit}\, e(\mathbf{L}) = \alpha_0 + \alpha_1 \, \text{Age} + \alpha_2 \, \text{Sex}$$ For a 70-year-old male, this might yield $\hat{e}(\mathbf{L}) = 0.78$. For a 30-year-old female, $\hat{e}(\mathbf{L}) = 0.22$.

3.3 Creating a pseudo-population

Now here is the key step. Consider a treated 70-year-old male with propensity score 0.78. We give him a weight of $1 / 0.78 \approx 1.28$. Why? Because in the original data, 70-year-old males are overrepresented among the treated (they had a high probability of being treated). By weighting him by the inverse of this probability, we effectively say: "this person represents not just himself, but also those similar people who, by chance of clinical decision, did not receive treatment."

Similarly, consider an untreated 30-year-old female with propensity score 0.22. Her probability of being untreated is $1 - 0.22 = 0.78$. We give her a weight of $1 / 0.78 \approx 1.28$. She was very likely to be untreated, so she doesn't need much upweighting. But a 70-year-old male who happened not to receive treatment (propensity score 0.78, so probability of being untreated = 0.22) gets a weight of $1/0.22 \approx 4.55$—he is rare and informative.

The intuition

The weights create a pseudo-population in which treatment assignment is independent of the confounders—as if the data came from a randomized experiment. In this pseudo-population, age and sex are equally distributed between treated and untreated groups. The confounding is removed.

3.4 Visualizing the reweighting

Figure 1.1 — The confounders L (age, sex) affect both treatment A and outcome Y, creating a backdoor path $A \leftarrow L \rightarrow Y$ that biases the crude association.

The logic of IPW can be understood through this diagram. The arrows $L \to A$ and $L \to Y$ create a backdoor path between treatment and outcome that flows through the confounders. The crude association between $A$ and $Y$ is a mixture of the causal effect ($A \to Y$) and the spurious association induced by this backdoor path.

What IPW does, conceptually, is sever the arrow from $L$ to $A$. By reweighting the data so that treatment is no longer associated with the confounders, we create a pseudo-population where the only remaining path from $A$ to $Y$ is the causal one. In the language of Hernán and Robins (2020), we are emulating a target trial in which treatment was randomly assigned.

4. The Math

Prerequisites from Module 0

This section assumes you have read the Foundations module. In particular: notation ($A$, $Y$, $\mathbf{L}$, $Y^a$, $\psi$, $e(\mathbf{L})$), the three identification assumptions (exchangeability, positivity, consistency), and the identification result: $\psi = \E_{\mathbf{L}}[\E[Y \mid A\!=\!1, \mathbf{L}]] - \E_{\mathbf{L}}[\E[Y \mid A\!=\!0, \mathbf{L}]]$. We now derive IPW as a specific estimation strategy for this identified quantity.

4.1 The IPW estimator

Rather than modeling the outcome, IPW uses a representation of $\E[Y^a]$ that involves the propensity score. The key identity is:

$$ \E[Y^a] = \E\!\left[\frac{\mathbb{1}(A = a) \, Y}{f(A \mid \mathbf{L})}\right] $$

where $f(A \mid \mathbf{L})$ is the conditional probability of the treatment actually received. For binary treatment, this means $f(A = 1 \mid \mathbf{L}) = e(\mathbf{L})$ and $f(A = 0 \mid \mathbf{L}) = 1 - e(\mathbf{L})$.

To see why this holds, consider the treated case ($a = 1$):

$$ \begin{aligned} \E\!\left[\frac{A \cdot Y}{e(\mathbf{L})}\right] &= \E_{\mathbf{L}}\!\left[\E\!\left[\frac{A \cdot Y}{e(\mathbf{L})} \,\Big|\, \mathbf{L}\right]\right] \\[6pt] &= \E_{\mathbf{L}}\!\left[\frac{1}{e(\mathbf{L})} \, \E[A \cdot Y \mid \mathbf{L}]\right] \\[6pt] &= \E_{\mathbf{L}}\!\left[\frac{1}{e(\mathbf{L})} \, \P(A=1 \mid \mathbf{L}) \, \E[Y \mid A=1, \mathbf{L}]\right] \\[6pt] &= \E_{\mathbf{L}}\!\Big[\E[Y \mid A=1, \mathbf{L}]\Big] \\[6pt] &= \E[Y^1] \end{aligned} $$

The propensity score cancels with the conditional probability of treatment, leaving exactly the G-formula identification result. But the estimation strategy is different: instead of modeling $\E[Y \mid A, \mathbf{L}]$, we model $\P(A = 1 \mid \mathbf{L})$.

This gives us the Horvitz-Thompson IPW estimator of the ATE:

$$ \hat{\psi}_{\text{HT}} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{A_i \, Y_i}{\hat{e}(\mathbf{L}_i)} \;-\; \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{(1 - A_i) \, Y_i}{1 - \hat{e}(\mathbf{L}_i)} $$

4.2 The Hájek (stabilized) estimator

The Horvitz-Thompson estimator can be numerically unstable when propensity scores are close to 0 or 1, because the weights $1/\hat{e}(\mathbf{L})$ become very large. A more stable variant normalizes the weights so they sum to 1 within each treatment group:

$$ \hat{\psi}_{\text{Hájek}} = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{A_i \, Y_i}{\hat{e}(\mathbf{L}_i)}}{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{A_i}{\hat{e}(\mathbf{L}_i)}} \;-\; \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{(1-A_i) \, Y_i}{1-\hat{e}(\mathbf{L}_i)}}{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{(1-A_i)}{1-\hat{e}(\mathbf{L}_i)}} $$

This is equivalent to computing the weighted mean outcome among treated (with weights $1/\hat{e}$) minus the weighted mean among untreated (with weights $1/(1-\hat{e})$). The Hájek estimator is a ratio estimator and hence only asymptotically unbiased, but it typically has smaller variance than the Horvitz-Thompson in finite samples and is the version more commonly used in practice.

4.3 Stabilized weights

An alternative approach to reducing variance is to use stabilized weights (Hernán & Robins, 2020; Robins et al., 2000). Instead of weighting by $1 / \P(A \mid \mathbf{L})$, we weight by:

$$ SW_i = \frac{\P(A_i)}{P(A_i \mid \mathbf{L}_i)} $$

where $\P(A_i)$ is the marginal probability of the treatment actually received. For a treated person, this is $\P(A=1) / e(\mathbf{L}_i)$; for an untreated person, $\P(A=0) / (1 - e(\mathbf{L}_i))$.

The numerator does not affect consistency of the estimator (the propensity score in the denominator is what removes confounding), but it brings the weights closer to 1 on average, reducing their variability. In a correctly specified model, stabilized weights sum to approximately $N$ in the full sample, whereas unstabilized weights can have a much larger or smaller sum. This is particularly useful in practice for fitting weighted regression models (marginal structural models).

4.4 What IPW is really doing

Let us step back and see the bigger picture. We can write the IPW estimator compactly using the general form:

$$ \hat{\psi}_{\text{IPW}} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left[\frac{A_i}{\hat{e}(\mathbf{L}_i)} - \frac{1 - A_i}{1 - \hat{e}(\mathbf{L}_i)}\right] Y_i $$

Each observation $i$ contributes to the estimate, but its contribution is reweighted by how surprising its treatment status is given its covariates. A treated person with a low propensity score (someone who was unlikely to be treated but was) gets a large weight—they are rare and informative. A treated person with a high propensity score (someone who was very likely to be treated) gets a small weight—they are expected and less informative.

This is the deep connection to survey sampling: just as the Horvitz-Thompson estimator in survey statistics corrects for unequal sampling probabilities, the IPW estimator corrects for unequal treatment probabilities. The analogy is precise—confounding is a sampling problem, where nature "sampled" people into treatment and control groups with probabilities that depend on their characteristics.

Summary so far

IPW estimates the causal effect by modeling the treatment assignment mechanism rather than the outcome. Under the identification assumptions, the IPW estimator is consistent for the ATE when the propensity score model is correctly specified—regardless of the true form of the outcome model.

But this is also its vulnerability: if the propensity score model is wrong, the IPW estimate will generally be biased. The next section explores exactly how and when this happens.

5. What Breaks

IPW can fail in several distinct ways. Understanding these failure modes is essential both for responsible application and for motivating the more robust estimators we will encounter in later modules.

5.1 Propensity score misspecification

The IPW estimator is consistent only when the propensity score model $e(\mathbf{L})$ is correctly specified. In practice, "correctly specified" means that the fitted model captures the true relationship between the confounders and treatment assignment, including any nonlinearities and interactions.

Return to our running example. Suppose that the true treatment assignment depends on age in a nonlinear way—perhaps through $\exp(\text{Age}/2)$ rather than Age itself—and we fit a logistic regression with Age entered linearly. The estimated propensity scores will be wrong, and the resulting weights will not correctly remove confounding. The IPW estimate will be biased, and this bias does not shrink with larger sample sizes. More data cannot fix a wrong model.

Naimi, Mishler & Kennedy (2023) demonstrated this vividly in a simulation study. They generated data from a known model with four confounders, then deliberately fitted the propensity score to transformed versions of those confounders (e.g., $Z_1 = \exp(C_1/2)$ instead of $C_1$). Under this misspecification, the IPW estimator showed substantial bias that persisted even at $n = 5{,}000$. The G-computation estimator was equally biased under its own form of misspecification. Only the doubly robust estimators (AIPW, TMLE) offered partial protection—a preview of Module 3.

Lesson

IPW places all its eggs in one basket: the propensity score model. If that model is wrong, there is no safety net. The bias is of the same order as the bias from a misspecified outcome regression. IPW and G-computation are "singly robust"—each depends on getting one model right.

5.2 Positivity violations and extreme weights

Even when the propensity score model is correctly specified, IPW can behave poorly in practice when positivity is near-violated: that is, when some individuals have propensity scores very close to 0 or 1.

Consider a 85-year-old male in our running example. If almost all such patients receive the drug, the estimated propensity score might be $\hat{e}(\mathbf{L}) = 0.98$. If this patient happens not to be treated, his weight is $1/(1 - 0.98) = 50$. This single individual now counts as 50 people in the pseudo-population. A single outlier can dominate the entire estimate.

The consequences are twofold. First, high variance: the estimate becomes unstable across samples because a few extreme weights drive the result. Second, finite-sample bias: even though the estimator is asymptotically unbiased, in any realistic sample the extreme weights introduce erratic behavior. Austin & Stuart (2017) showed in simulations that when the treatment-selection process is strong (i.e., confounders strongly predict treatment), both IPTW and matching produced biased estimates of marginal hazard ratios, even with a correctly specified propensity score model.

5.3 Practical diagnostics and remedies

Several practical strategies can mitigate (but not eliminate) these problems:

Weight truncation (trimming). Cap the weights at some percentile (e.g., the 1st and 99th percentiles) to limit the influence of extreme observations. This introduces a small bias but can dramatically reduce variance. The trade-off depends on the severity of the positivity violation.

Stabilized weights. As discussed in Section 4.3, using $SW = \P(A) / \P(A \mid \mathbf{L})$ instead of $1/\P(A \mid \mathbf{L})$ reduces weight variability without affecting consistency.

Covariate balance checking. After weighting, verify that the weighted distribution of confounders is similar between treated and untreated groups. Common diagnostics include standardized mean differences (SMD) before and after weighting; the target is typically $|\text{SMD}| < 0.1$.

Propensity score distribution overlap. Plot the distributions of $\hat{e}(\mathbf{L})$ in both treatment groups. If there are regions where one group has density but the other does not, positivity is violated there, and the IPW estimate for those subgroups is unreliable.

5.4 Unmeasured confounding

Finally, and most fundamentally: IPW (like all methods in this series that rely on the exchangeability assumption) cannot correct for confounders that are not measured. If a variable affects both treatment and outcome but is absent from $\mathbf{L}$, the propensity score cannot account for it, and the estimate will be biased regardless of how well the propensity score model fits the observed data.

This is not a limitation specific to IPW—it applies equally to G-computation, AIPW, and TMLE. No statistical method can substitute for a well-designed study with adequate confounder measurement. The identification assumptions are assumptions about the world, not about the estimator.

6. Implementation

We now implement the IPW estimator on our running example. We first simulate the data from a known data-generating process so that we can verify that IPW recovers the true causal effect, then show the implementation in both R and Python.

6.1 Data-generating process

We use the shared DGP defined in Module 0:

$$ \begin{aligned} \text{Age} &\sim \text{Uniform}(25,\, 75) \\[4pt] \text{Sex} &\sim \text{Bernoulli}(0.5) \quad (\text{1 = male, 0 = female})\\[4pt] \P(A = 1 \mid \mathbf{L}) &= \text{expit}(-2 + 0.03 \cdot \text{Age} + 0.5 \cdot \text{Sex}) \\[4pt] \P(Y = 1 \mid A, \mathbf{L}) &= \text{expit}(-1.5 + 0.04 \cdot \text{Age} + 0.3 \cdot \text{Sex} - 0.8 \cdot A) \end{aligned} $$

The true causal effect of $A$ on $Y$ is a risk difference of approximately $-0.14$ (the drug reduces disease risk by about 14 percentage points). The coefficient $-0.8$ on the log-odds scale translates to this risk difference after averaging over the confounder distribution. Older age and male sex both increase treatment probability and disease risk, creating confounding that reverses the apparent effect if unaccounted for.

6.2 R implementation

# ── Simulate data ──────────────────────────────────────
set.seed(42)
n <- 10000
age <- runif(n, 25, 75)
sex <- rbinom(n, 1, 0.5)

expit <- function(x) 1 / (1 + exp(-x))

# Treatment model (true propensity score)
ps_true <- expit(-2 + 0.03 * age + 0.5 * sex)
A <- rbinom(n, 1, ps_true)

# Outcome model
p_y <- expit(-1.5 + 0.04 * age + 0.3 * sex - 0.8 * A)
Y <- rbinom(n, 1, p_y)

# ── Estimate propensity scores ─────────────────────────
ps_model <- glm(A ~ age + sex, family = binomial)
ps_hat <- predict(ps_model, type = "response")

# ── Naive (unadjusted) estimate ────────────────────────
naive <- mean(Y[A == 1]) - mean(Y[A == 0])
cat("Naive estimate:", round(naive, 4), "\n")

# ── Horvitz-Thompson IPW estimator ─────────────────────
ipw_ht <- mean(A * Y / ps_hat) - mean((1 - A) * Y / (1 - ps_hat))
cat("IPW (Horvitz-Thompson):", round(ipw_ht, 4), "\n")

# ── Hájek (normalized) IPW estimator ───────────────────
ipw_hajek <- ( sum(A * Y / ps_hat) / sum(A / ps_hat) ) -
              ( sum((1-A) * Y / (1-ps_hat)) / sum((1-A) / (1-ps_hat)) )
cat("IPW (Hájek):", round(ipw_hajek, 4), "\n")

# ── Diagnostics: covariate balance ─────────────────────
w1 <- A / ps_hat                   # weights for treated
w0 <- (1 - A) / (1 - ps_hat)      # weights for untreated

smd_age_before <- (mean(age[A==1]) - mean(age[A==0])) / sd(age)
smd_age_after  <- (weighted.mean(age, w1 * A) -
                    weighted.mean(age, w0 * (1-A))) / sd(age)

cat("SMD (age) before weighting:", round(smd_age_before, 3), "\n")
cat("SMD (age) after weighting: ", round(smd_age_after, 3), "\n")

6.3 Python implementation

# ── Simulate data ──────────────────────────────────────
import numpy as np
from scipy.special import expit
from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression

np.random.seed(42)
n = 10_000
age = np.random.uniform(25, 75, n)
sex = np.random.binomial(1, 0.5, n)

# Treatment model (true propensity score)
ps_true = expit(-2 + 0.03 * age + 0.5 * sex)
A = np.random.binomial(1, ps_true)

# Outcome model
p_y = expit(-1.5 + 0.04 * age + 0.3 * sex - 0.8 * A)
Y = np.random.binomial(1, p_y)

# ── Estimate propensity scores ─────────────────────────
L = np.column_stack([age, sex])
ps_model = LogisticRegression(max_iter=1000)
ps_model.fit(L, A)
ps_hat = ps_model.predict_proba(L)[:, 1]

# ── Naive (unadjusted) estimate ────────────────────────
naive = Y[A == 1].mean() - Y[A == 0].mean()
print(f"Naive estimate: {naive:.4f}")

# ── Horvitz-Thompson IPW estimator ─────────────────────
ipw_ht = np.mean(A * Y / ps_hat) - np.mean((1 - A) * Y / (1 - ps_hat))
print(f"IPW (Horvitz-Thompson): {ipw_ht:.4f}")

# ── Hájek (normalized) IPW estimator ───────────────────
ipw_hajek = ( np.sum(A * Y / ps_hat) / np.sum(A / ps_hat) -
              np.sum((1-A) * Y / (1-ps_hat)) / np.sum((1-A) / (1-ps_hat)) )
print(f"IPW (Hájek): {ipw_hajek:.4f}")

# ── Diagnostics: covariate balance ─────────────────────
w1 = A / ps_hat
w0 = (1 - A) / (1 - ps_hat)

smd_before = (age[A==1].mean() - age[A==0].mean()) / age.std()
smd_after  = ( np.average(age, weights=w1*A) -
               np.average(age, weights=w0*(1-A)) ) / age.std()

print(f"SMD (age) before weighting: {smd_before:.3f}")
print(f"SMD (age) after weighting:  {smd_after:.3f}")
Expected output

The naive estimate will be positive (around +0.04 to +0.08), wrongly suggesting the drug increases disease risk. Both IPW estimators should recover approximately $-0.14$, close to the true ATE. The SMD for age should drop from around 0.20–0.30 (before weighting) to near 0 (after weighting), confirming that the pseudo-population is balanced.

7. Takeaway

7.1 What we learned

Inverse probability weighting estimates causal effects by modeling the treatment assignment mechanism rather than the outcome. It creates a pseudo-population in which confounders are balanced between treatment groups, emulating a randomized experiment. When the propensity score model is correctly specified and positivity holds, the IPW estimator is consistent for the ATE.

Its main vulnerability is that it depends entirely on one model being right: the propensity score. Misspecification leads to bias that does not disappear with more data. Near-violations of positivity cause extreme weights that inflate variance and can make the estimate erratic in finite samples.

7.3 Bridge to Module 2

What comes next

IPW models the treatment. The G-formula takes the opposite approach: it models the outcome $\E[Y \mid A, \mathbf{L}]$ and uses it to predict what would happen under each treatment level. It is equally powerful and equally fragile—just in a different direction. Understanding both is necessary to appreciate why, in Module 3, we combine them into the doubly robust AIPW estimator that protects against misspecification of either model (but not both).
