Separable Effects — The Solution

Part 2: Stensrud, Young, Didelez, Robins & Hernán (2020) proposed a causal effect that does not require eliminating death.

This post explains Stensrud, Young, Didelez, Robins and Hernán (2020), Separable effects for causal inference in the presence of competing events. It is the second in a series. Part 1 covered Young et al. (2020), which diagnosed the problem: the total effect is ambiguous when treatment affects the competing event, and the direct effect requires the impossible elimination of a terminal event. This paper fills the gap.

1. A different question

The direct effect of Young et al. asks: what would happen if we could eliminate death from other causes? Stensrud et al. ask something different: what would happen if we gave a different drug?

DES (estrogen therapy) does two things biologically. It suppresses testosterone, which reduces prostate cancer death. And it increases coagulation factors, which increases cardiovascular death. These are distinct biochemical pathways packaged in the same molecule. The paper labels them:

  • $\color{teal}{A_Y}$ — the component that directly affects the event of interest (testosterone suppression)
  • $\color{orange}{A_D}$ — the component that directly affects the competing event (coagulation effect)

The question becomes: what would happen if we gave a drug that has the $\color{teal}{A_Y}$ mechanism (testosterone suppression) but not the $\color{orange}{A_D}$ mechanism (no coagulation harm)? Such drugs exist — LHRH antagonists suppress testosterone without the cardiovascular toxicity of estrogen. The estimand corresponds to a real intervention.

Figure 1 — DES ($A$) is decomposed into $\color{teal}{A_Y}$ (testosterone suppression → prostate cancer death) and $\color{orange}{A_D}$ (coagulation → cardiovascular death). The arrow $D_k \to Y_k$ is structural: death from cardiovascular disease precludes prostate cancer death. The separable direct effect isolates $\color{teal}{A_Y} \to Y_k$; the separable indirect effect captures $\color{orange}{A_D} \to D_k \to Y_k$.

Setting $A_D = 0$ is not eliminating death

Setting $\color{orange}{A_D} = 0$ means setting the competing-event component of treatment to its placebo value. People can still die of cardiovascular disease — they die at the rate they would under placebo. Nobody becomes immortal. The intervention removes the treatment's effect on cardiovascular death, not cardiovascular death itself. This is what distinguishes the separable effect from the direct effect of Young et al., which requires the impossible elimination of a terminal event.

2. Why this solves the ambiguity

The total effect's problem: the competing event rate differs between arms. DES increases cardiovascular death, which mechanically reduces the number of people who can die of prostate cancer. The apparent benefit on prostate cancer might be an artefact.

The separable direct effect compares:

$$\P[Y^{\color{teal}{a_Y=1},\, \color{orange}{a_D=0}}_{k+1} = 1] \;-\; \P[Y^{\color{teal}{a_Y=0},\, \color{orange}{a_D=0}}_{k+1} = 1]$$

In both worlds, $\color{orange}{a_D = 0}$. The competing event hazard is at placebo rate in both arms of the comparison. The only thing that differs is $\color{teal}{a_Y}$ — the component that directly affects prostate cancer. Any difference in prostate cancer risk between these two worlds cannot be an artefact of differential cardiovascular mortality, because the cardiovascular mortality is held constant.

The separable indirect effect captures the remainder:

$$\P[Y^{\color{teal}{a_Y=1},\, \color{orange}{a_D=1}}_{k+1} = 1] \;-\; \P[Y^{\color{teal}{a_Y=1},\, \color{orange}{a_D=0}}_{k+1} = 1]$$

Here $\color{teal}{a_Y}$ is held fixed while $\color{orange}{a_D}$ varies. This measures how much adding the cardiovascular harm changes the prostate cancer risk — the mediation pathway through the competing event.

Together, the two effects decompose the total effect into the part that operates directly on the event of interest and the part that operates through the competing event.

3. The decomposition assumption

In the observed data, every patient receives the whole drug or no drug: $A \equiv \color{teal}{A_Y} \equiv \color{orange}{A_D}$. We only observe $(a_Y = 1, a_D = 1)$ and $(a_Y = 0, a_D = 0)$, never the cross-arms. The decomposition is not a data operation — it is a claim about the biological structure of the treatment.

Decomposition assumption (equation 2)

The treatment $A$ can be decomposed into components $\color{teal}{A_Y}$ and $\color{orange}{A_D}$ such that $A \equiv \color{teal}{A_Y} \equiv \color{orange}{A_D}$ in the observed data, but in a future study, $\color{teal}{A_Y}$ and $\color{orange}{A_D}$ could in principle be assigned different values. Setting both to the same value recovers the original treatment: $$Y^{\color{teal}{a_Y} = a,\, \color{orange}{a_D} = a} \;=\; Y^{A = a}$$

The assumption is not testable in our current two-arm trial. But it would be testable in a hypothetical four-arm trial that separately randomises $\color{teal}{A_Y}$ and $\color{orange}{A_D}$. In the DES example, the four arms would be:

$\color{teal}{A_Y}$$\color{orange}{A_D}$InterventionObserved?
11Full DES (testosterone suppression + coagulation)Yes — the DES arm
00Placebo (no suppression, no coagulation)Yes — the placebo arm
10Testosterone suppression only (e.g. LHRH antagonist)No — but could exist
01Coagulation effect only (e.g. DES + exogenous testosterone)No — but could exist

The paper states this explicitly: "the separable effect estimates obtained from (8) in our current study can be confirmed" in such a trial. The cross-arms ($\color{teal}{a_Y} \neq \color{orange}{a_D}$) correspond to drugs that could, in principle, be manufactured and tested. The decomposition assumption says these interventions are well-defined.

4. The dismissible component conditions

Two conditions are required for identification — the bridge from counterfactual quantities (which we cannot observe) to functions of observed data (which we can estimate). Without identification, the separable effects are well-defined but not computable from a two-arm trial.

Both conditions are stated in terms of the cause-specific hazard — the instantaneous risk of an event among individuals who are alive and free of both events at time $k$, conditional on covariates $L$.

$\Delta_1$: $\color{orange}{A_D}$ is dismissible from the $Y$ hazard

$$\P[Y^{\color{teal}{a_Y},\, \color{orange}{a_D=1}}_{k+1} = 1 \mid Y^{\color{teal}{a_Y},\, \color{orange}{a_D=1}}_k = 0,\; D^{\color{teal}{a_Y},\, \color{orange}{a_D=1}}_{k+1} = 0,\; L = l]$$ $$= \P[Y^{\color{teal}{a_Y},\, \color{orange}{a_D=0}}_{k+1} = 1 \mid Y^{\color{teal}{a_Y},\, \color{orange}{a_D=0}}_k = 0,\; D^{\color{teal}{a_Y},\, \color{orange}{a_D=0}}_{k+1} = 0,\; L = l]$$ Among event-free individuals with covariates $L = l$, the cause-specific hazard of the event of interest does not depend on the value of $\color{orange}{A_D}$. In words: the coagulation effect of DES does not change your prostate cancer risk.

$\Delta_2$: $\color{teal}{A_Y}$ is dismissible from the $D$ hazard

$$\P[D^{\color{teal}{a_Y=1},\, \color{orange}{a_D}}_{k+1} = 1 \mid Y^{\color{teal}{a_Y=1},\, \color{orange}{a_D}}_k = 0,\; D^{\color{teal}{a_Y=1},\, \color{orange}{a_D}}_k = 0,\; L = l]$$ $$= \P[D^{\color{teal}{a_Y=0},\, \color{orange}{a_D}}_{k+1} = 1 \mid Y^{\color{teal}{a_Y=0},\, \color{orange}{a_D}}_k = 0,\; D^{\color{teal}{a_Y=0},\, \color{orange}{a_D}}_k = 0,\; L = l]$$ Among event-free individuals with covariates $L = l$, the cause-specific hazard of the competing event does not depend on the value of $\color{teal}{A_Y}$. In words: the testosterone suppression of DES does not change your cardiovascular death risk.

Together: each hazard depends on only one component of treatment. The $Y$ hazard depends on $\color{teal}{A_Y}$ only, the $D$ hazard depends on $\color{orange}{A_D}$ only, conditional on being event-free and on $L$.

What violates them. An unmeasured common cause $U_{YD}$ that affects both prostate cancer risk and cardiovascular death risk. If such a variable exists, conditioning on being event-free ($D_{k+1} = 0$) opens a collider path: $\color{orange}{A_D} \to D_{k+1} \leftarrow U_{YD} \to Y_{k+1}$. This makes $\color{orange}{A_D}$ appear to affect $Y$ even when it does not biologically — the dismissible condition $\Delta_1$ fails.

What does not violate them. Separate unmeasured causes of $Y$ alone or $D$ alone. A genetic factor $U_Y$ that affects only prostate cancer risk creates no collider path when conditioning on $D_{k+1} = 0$, because $U_Y$ does not cause $D$.

These conditions are claims about biology, not statistical properties of the data. They are untestable in a two-arm trial but would be testable in a four-arm trial where $\color{teal}{A_Y}$ and $\color{orange}{A_D}$ are separately randomised.

5. How identification works

Starting from the target quantity, each line follows from the one above.

$$\P[Y^{\color{teal}{a_Y},\, \color{orange}{a_D}}_{K+1} = 1] \qquad \small{\text{target}}$$ $$= \sum_l \sum_{s=0}^{K} \P[Y^{\color{teal}{a_Y},\, \color{orange}{a_D}}_{s+1} = 1,\; D^{\color{teal}{a_Y},\, \color{orange}{a_D}}_{s+1} = 0,\; Y^{\color{teal}{a_Y},\, \color{orange}{a_D}}_s = 0 \mid L = l]\; \P[L = l] \qquad \small{\text{sum over first-event time}}$$

Every term depends on both $\color{teal}{a_Y}$ and $\color{orange}{a_D}$ — unobservable when $\color{teal}{a_Y} \neq \color{orange}{a_D}$. The intermediate steps below use the chain rule and the dismissible conditions to separate the two components.

Show intermediate steps (chain rule → dismissible conditions → decomposition)

Apply the chain rule to factor "event at $s+1$ and event-free through $s$" into a hazard times a product of event-free probabilities:

$$= \sum_l \sum_{s=0}^{K} \P[Y^{\color{teal}{a_Y},\, \color{orange}{a_D}}_{s+1} = 1 \mid D^{\color{teal}{a_Y},\, \color{orange}{a_D}}_{s+1} = 0,\; Y^{\color{teal}{a_Y},\, \color{orange}{a_D}}_s = 0,\; L = l] \times \prod_{j=0}^{s} \left( \P[D^{\color{teal}{a_Y},\, \color{orange}{a_D}}_{j+1} = 0 \mid \ldots] \times \P[Y^{\color{teal}{a_Y},\, \color{orange}{a_D}}_j = 0 \mid \ldots] \right) \P[L = l] \qquad \small{\text{chain rule}}$$

Apply $\Delta_1$: the $Y$ hazard and $Y$ event-free terms do not depend on $\color{orange}{a_D}$, so set $\color{orange}{a_D} = \color{teal}{a_Y}$. Apply $\Delta_2$: the $D$ event-free term does not depend on $\color{teal}{a_Y}$, so set $\color{teal}{a_Y} = \color{orange}{a_D}$:

$$= \sum_l \sum_{s=0}^{K} \P[Y^{\color{teal}{a_Y},\, \color{orange}{a_D = a_Y}}_{s+1} = 1 \mid \ldots] \times \prod_{j=0}^{s} \left( \P[D^{\color{teal}{a_Y = a_D},\, \color{orange}{a_D}}_{j+1} = 0 \mid \ldots] \times \P[Y^{\color{teal}{a_Y},\, \color{orange}{a_D = a_Y}}_j = 0 \mid \ldots] \right) \P[L = l] \qquad \small{\Delta_1, \Delta_2}$$

By the decomposition assumption, setting both components to the same value recovers a single-world counterfactual: $Y^{\color{teal}{a_Y}, \color{orange}{a_D} = \color{teal}{a_Y}} = Y^{a = \color{teal}{a_Y}}$ and $D^{\color{teal}{a_Y} = \color{orange}{a_D}, \color{orange}{a_D}} = D^{a = \color{orange}{a_D}}$:

$$= \sum_l \sum_{s=0}^{K} \P[Y^{a = \color{teal}{a_Y}}_{s+1} = 1 \mid \ldots] \times \prod_{j=0}^{s} \left( \P[D^{a = \color{orange}{a_D}}_{j+1} = 0 \mid \ldots] \times \P[Y^{a = \color{teal}{a_Y}}_j = 0 \mid \ldots] \right) \P[L = l] \qquad \small{\text{decomposition}}$$

Now, with the help of the dismissible component conditions, every term is a single-world counterfactual — under $A = \color{teal}{a_Y}$ or $A = \color{orange}{a_D}$, never both. Exchangeability and consistency replace them with observed data:

$$= \sum_l \sum_{s=0}^{K} \underbrace{\P[Y_{s+1} = 1 \mid D_{s+1} = Y_s = 0,\; A = \color{teal}{a_Y},\; L = l]}_{\text{CS hazard of } Y \text{ from } \color{teal}{a_Y} \text{ arm}} \prod_{j=0}^{s} \left( \underbrace{\P[D_{j+1} = 0 \mid D_j = Y_j = 0,\; A = \color{orange}{a_D},\; L = l]}_{\text{event-free from } D \text{ from } \color{orange}{a_D} \text{ arm}} \times \underbrace{\P[Y_j = 0 \mid D_j = Y_{j-1} = 0,\; A = \color{teal}{a_Y},\; L = l]}_{\text{event-free from } Y \text{ from } \color{teal}{a_Y} \text{ arm}} \right) \P[L = l] \qquad \small{\text{exchangeability + consistency}}$$

This is the g-formula (equation 8). Everything related to $Y$ comes from the $A = \color{teal}{a_Y}$ arm. The event-free probability from $D$ comes from the $A = \color{orange}{a_D}$ arm. When $\color{teal}{a_Y} = \color{orange}{a_D}$, all terms come from the same arm — the standard g-formula for the total effect. When $\color{teal}{a_Y} \neq \color{orange}{a_D}$, the formula constructs a quantity that never appears in either arm of the trial.

6. Limitations and forward pointers

Full isolation assumed. The 2020 paper assumes that $\color{teal}{A_Y}$ does not affect any intermediate variable that also causes $D$, and vice versa. When treatment operates through shared intermediate variables — for example, a blood-pressure-lowering drug where blood pressure affects both the event of interest and the competing event — this assumption fails. Stensrud et al. (2021) generalise the framework to handle time-varying covariates, partial isolation, and shared intermediates. Part 3 covers this.

Baseline covariates only. The identification formula above conditions on baseline $L$ only. When time-varying confounders are affected by treatment, the g-formula must account for their evolving distribution — requiring additional models and Monte Carlo simulation. This is also addressed in the 2021 generalisation.

Estimation details deferred. The technical companion post will walk through the estimators (g-formula, IPW).

The dismissible conditions are untestable in a two-arm trial. They are biological claims about how the treatment operates, not statistical properties that can be checked against data. The 2021 paper provides a sensitivity analysis framework for investigating the consequences of their violation.
