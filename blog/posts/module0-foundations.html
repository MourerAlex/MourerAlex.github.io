Causal Inference Methods — Module 0 of 6
2025-03-23 Causal Inference

Foundations

The language, assumptions, and logic that every method in this series shares.

1. Why a Foundations Module?

Every method in this series—IPW, G-formula, AIPW, the one-step estimator, TMLE—is a different estimation strategy for the same causal quantity, under the same set of assumptions, expressed in the same notation. If we repeated the notation, estimand definition, and assumptions inside each module, three things would happen: redundancy, inconsistency risk, and the false impression that the methods differ more fundamentally than they do.

This module lays down the shared foundation once. Every subsequent module will open with a brief recall box pointing back here, then move directly to what is specific to that method.

2. The Setup

We observe data on $n$ individuals from a population. For each individual, we record three things: a treatment they received, an outcome we care about, and a set of pre-treatment variables that might confound the relationship between the two.

Running Example — used across all modules

A public health agency wants to know whether a preventive drug reduces the risk of a disease (binary: yes/no). They have observational data from a large cohort. Two variables were measured at baseline: age (continuous, in years) and sex (binary: male/female). No randomized trial is available.

In an ideal world, we would conduct a randomized experiment: flip a coin for each person, give the drug to those who get heads, and compare disease rates. Randomization ensures that, on average, the treated and untreated groups are comparable in every way—measured or unmeasured. Any difference in outcomes can be attributed to the drug.

But this is observational data. Physicians prescribed the drug based on clinical judgment. Older patients and male patients were more likely to receive the drug, because physicians perceived them as higher risk. The problem is that age and sex also independently affect disease risk. This creates confounding: the very factors that influence who gets treated also influence who gets sick.

Figure 0.1 — The confounders $\mathbf{L}$ (age, sex) affect both treatment $A$ and outcome $Y$, creating a backdoor path $A \leftarrow L \rightarrow Y$ that biases the crude association.

The arrows $L \to A$ and $L \to Y$ create a backdoor path between treatment and outcome. The crude association between $A$ and $Y$ is a mixture of the causal effect ($A \to Y$) and the spurious association flowing through this path. Every method in this series is a different way of blocking that backdoor path to isolate the causal effect.

3. Notation

We use the following notation throughout this series. It is consistent across all modules, so that the connections between methods remain clear.

Symbol Meaning
$A$ Binary treatment indicator ($A = 1$: treated, $A = 0$: untreated)
$Y$ Binary outcome ($Y = 1$: disease, $Y = 0$: no disease)
$\mathbf{L}$ Vector of measured confounders; here $\mathbf{L} = (\text{Age}, \text{Sex})$
$Y^a$ Potential (counterfactual) outcome under treatment $A = a$
$\psi$ Average treatment effect (ATE): $\psi = \E[Y^1] - \E[Y^0]$
$e(\mathbf{L})$ Propensity score: $\P(A = 1 \mid \mathbf{L})$
$\bar{Q}(A, \mathbf{L})$ Outcome regression: $\E[Y \mid A, \mathbf{L}]$

3.1 Potential outcomes

The potential outcomes framework (Neyman, 1923; Rubin, 1974) is the language we use to define causal effects. For each individual and each possible treatment value $a$, we posit a potential outcome $Y^a$: the outcome that would be observed if, possibly contrary to fact, the individual received treatment $a$.

Each person has two potential outcomes: $Y^1$ (outcome if treated) and $Y^0$ (outcome if untreated). We only ever observe one of them—the one corresponding to the treatment actually received. This is the fundamental problem of causal inference (Holland, 1986): individual causal effects $Y^1 - Y^0$ are never directly observable.

3.2 The estimand

Because we cannot observe individual effects, we target a population-level summary. Our target estimand is the average treatment effect (ATE), also known as the average causal effect or the causal risk difference:

$$\psi = \E[Y^1] - \E[Y^0] = \P(Y^1 = 1) - \P(Y^0 = 1)$$

This is the difference in disease risk that would be observed if the entire population were treated versus if the entire population were untreated. It is a contrast between two counterfactual worlds. In our running example, we will see that the true ATE is approximately $-0.14$: the drug reduces disease risk by about 14 percentage points.

Why population-level?

We cannot learn $Y^1_i - Y^0_i$ for any single person $i$, but we can learn the average $\E[Y^1 - Y^0] = \E[Y^1] - \E[Y^0]$ under certain assumptions. The rest of this module makes those assumptions precise.

4. Identification Assumptions

The ATE $\psi$ is defined in terms of potential outcomes $Y^1$ and $Y^0$, which are not simultaneously observable. To estimate $\psi$ from observational data, we need three structural assumptions that allow us to express the counterfactual quantity in terms of the observed data distribution. These assumptions are shared by every method in this series.

Assumption 1 — Exchangeability (no unmeasured confounding)

$$Y^a \ind A \mid \mathbf{L}, \quad \text{for } a \in \{0, 1\}$$ Conditional on the measured confounders $\mathbf{L}$, the potential outcomes are independent of the treatment actually received. Equivalently: within strata of $\mathbf{L}$, treatment assignment is "as good as random." This is untestable from the data alone and requires subject-matter knowledge.

Assumption 2 — Positivity (overlap)

$$0 < \P(A = 1 \mid \mathbf{L} = \mathbf{l}) < 1, \quad \text{for all } \mathbf{l} \text{ with } \P(\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{l}) > 0$$ Every individual in the population has a nonzero probability of receiving either treatment level. If some subgroup is never treated (or always treated), we cannot learn about the counterfactual for that subgroup. Positivity is empirically assessable, and we will see that violations cause serious practical problems.

Assumption 3 — Consistency

$$Y = Y^a \quad \text{when } A = a$$ The observed outcome for a person who received treatment $a$ equals their potential outcome under $a$. This links the counterfactual framework to observed data. It requires that there is a single, well-defined version of treatment, ruling out hidden variations in how the drug is administered.

4.1 What the assumptions buy us

Each assumption plays a distinct role. Exchangeability lets us condition on treatment without introducing selection bias. Consistency lets us replace potential outcomes with observed outcomes. Positivity ensures we have data in every corner of the covariate space.

Together, they are sometimes called the identifiability conditions or the no-confounding conditions. When people say a causal effect is "identified," they mean: under these assumptions, the causal quantity can be expressed as a function of the observed data distribution.

4.2 What they do not buy us

These assumptions are about the world, not about any particular estimator. They tell us that it is possible in principle to learn $\psi$ from the data. They say nothing about how to estimate it well. That is the job of the estimation methods in Modules 1–6.

Crucially, no statistical method can rescue you if these assumptions are violated. If there is unmeasured confounding, IPW, G-formula, AIPW, and TMLE will all be biased. The assumptions are prerequisites, not things the methods "handle."

5. The Identification Result

Under the three assumptions above, the counterfactual mean $\E[Y^a]$ can be expressed as a function of the observed data. The proof proceeds in three steps:

$$ \begin{aligned} \E[Y^a] &= \E_{\mathbf{L}}\!\Big[\E[Y^a \mid \mathbf{L}]\Big] && \text{(law of total expectation)} \\[6pt] &= \E_{\mathbf{L}}\!\Big[\E[Y^a \mid A = a, \mathbf{L}]\Big] && \text{(exchangeability: } Y^a \ind A \mid \mathbf{L}\text{)} \\[6pt] &= \E_{\mathbf{L}}\!\Big[\E[Y \mid A = a, \mathbf{L}]\Big] && \text{(consistency: } Y = Y^a \text{ when } A = a\text{)} \end{aligned} $$

The right-hand side involves only observed quantities: the conditional expectation of the observed outcome given treatment and confounders, averaged over the distribution of confounders. This is the identification of the causal parameter—we have translated a causal question into a statistical one.

The ATE is therefore:

$$\psi = \E_{\mathbf{L}}\!\Big[\E[Y \mid A = 1, \mathbf{L}]\Big] - \E_{\mathbf{L}}\!\Big[\E[Y \mid A = 0, \mathbf{L}]\Big]$$
The key equation

This expression is the starting point for every estimation method in this series. Each method is a different strategy for computing this quantity from a finite sample:

G-formula (Module 2): directly model $\E[Y \mid A, \mathbf{L}]$ and average the predictions.
IPW (Module 1): rewrite it using the propensity score and reweight the observed outcomes.
AIPW (Module 3): combine both approaches for double robustness.
TMLE (Module 6): use targeted bias reduction on the G-formula estimate.

Same estimand, same identification, different estimation.

6. The Simulation DGP

To ground everything in concrete numbers, we define a data-generating process that we will reuse across all modules. Because we control the DGP, we know the true causal effect and can verify that each estimator recovers it.

$$ \begin{aligned} \text{Age} &\sim \text{Uniform}(25,\, 75) \\[4pt] \text{Sex} &\sim \text{Bernoulli}(0.5) \quad (\text{1 = male, 0 = female})\\[4pt] \P(A = 1 \mid \mathbf{L}) &= \text{expit}(-2 + 0.03 \cdot \text{Age} + 0.5 \cdot \text{Sex}) \\[4pt] \P(Y = 1 \mid A, \mathbf{L}) &= \text{expit}(-1.5 + 0.04 \cdot \text{Age} + 0.3 \cdot \text{Sex} - 0.8 \cdot A) \end{aligned} $$

where $\text{expit}(x) = 1/(1 + e^{-x})$ is the inverse logit function.

The true causal effect of $A$ on $Y$ is a risk difference of approximately $\psi \approx -0.14$: the drug reduces disease risk by about 14 percentage points. The coefficient $-0.8$ on the log-odds scale translates to this risk difference after averaging over the confounder distribution.

Notice the confounding structure: older age and male sex both increase treatment probability and disease risk. A naive comparison of treated versus untreated will be confounded upward, making the drug appear harmful even though its true effect is protective. This is exactly the scenario we need to test whether each estimation method works.

Why this DGP?

It is simple enough to understand completely (two confounders, binary treatment, binary outcome, logistic models), yet rich enough to demonstrate all the issues: confounding, propensity score estimation, model misspecification, and the differences between estimators. Every module will simulate from this same DGP, so results are directly comparable.

7. Road Map

With notation, assumptions, and the identification result in hand, here is how the rest of the series is organized. Each module tackles one estimation strategy for the same identified ATE:
