The Causal Roadmap

A step-by-step guide to defining the study design.

The Causal Roadmap (van der Laan & Rose, 2011) was developed by Mark van der Laan and team. It organises the design of a causal study into six interconnected steps. The Roadmap can help investigators to prespecify design and analysis plans for studies that utilise real-world data (RWD), choose between study designs, and propose high-quality real-world evidence (RWE) studies to the FDA and other agencies.

This framework has been formalised conjointly with two companion frameworks (Dang et al., 2023): the ICH E9(R1) Estimand Framework and Target Trial Emulation (Hernán & Robins, 2016). Each can be used separately or jointly; together they help define the components of an estimand, a causal question, and an analysis plan via clear specification.

We illustrate the roadmap with a running example drawn from pharmacoepidemiology.

1. Study Objective

Start from the scientific question. What effect, in what population, over what time horizon?

Example

The real-world effectiveness of study drug vs. comparator in patients with T2D for endpoint X.

The study objective drives every subsequent choice. It must be stated before looking at the data, to avoid letting data availability dictate the scientific question.

2. Data Collection

Characterise the data source and assess whether it can support the study objective. Key questions:

Database characteristics — what type of data (e.g. claims, EHR, registry)? What population does it capture?
Eligibility criteria reproducible — can the target population be reproduced from the available variables?
Outcomes observability — is the endpoint of interest captured reliably?
Confounders availability — are the key confounders measured?
Treatment information — is there sufficient detail (prescription date, pickup date, dose) to reconstruct treatment strategies?

Decision checkpoint

Does the data support the current design? If yes: proceed to step 3. If no: revisit the study objective or seek a different data source.

3. Design Methods

The design choice must address three key challenges:

Establishing time zero — when does follow-up start for each individual?
Assigning individuals to a unique treatment strategy at time zero — each person must be uniquely assigned to one strategy at the start of follow-up.
Enough individuals initiating each treatment strategy of interest — sample size, given eligibility criteria and database, must be deemed sufficient for running the analysis.

The attainment of these three components, with the help of statistical methods to form comparable groups, will mark the time—and achievement—of emulated randomisation.

Verification at this stage includes: establishing time zero and assigning treatment groups, checking the number of participants by treatment group and adherence pattern, and confirming that patient characteristics are in line with the study objective.

4. Causal Question

The causal question must be phrased as a precise counterfactual contrast. A useful template:

Structured causal question

What would be the X-year risk ratio of all-cause death if, at start of follow-up, all eligible patients (list conditions) had initiated and always been treated with study drug versus comparator, as though patients never… (per-protocol / hypothetical), regardless of… (as-assigned / treatment policy)?

The best way to answer this question is to evaluate the potential outcomes under both treatment strategies, with a structural causal model describing the generation of those potential outcomes.

Both the ICH E9(R1) and Target Trial Emulation frameworks prompt investigators to define the components of the causal question and estimand via clear specification. The table below shows how their elements align:

5. Causal Model

Encode the data-generating process—the study type (e.g. pragmatic randomised trial, retrospective cohort study) and the factors that affect treatment strategies and outcome—as well as potential mediators, colliders, and confounders: nonrandomised factors that may affect both the treatment/exposure and the outcome. Sources for this step include database knowledge, medical assumptions, and treatment guidelines.

Figure 1 — A simplified causal model. Measured covariates affect both treatment and outcome. The dashed arrows represent potential unknown confounders whose presence would threaten exchangeability.

The causal model makes identifiability conditions explicit. Two key conditions must hold for the population summary to be identifiable from the data:

Exchangeability

No unmeasured confounders affect both treatment and outcome, and no conditioning on mediators.

Positivity

Every individual has a positive probability of following any treatment strategy, given their measured characteristics.

Decision checkpoint

Is the population summary identifiable? If yes: proceed to estimation. If no: revisit the causal model, reassess confounders, or plan a sensitivity analysis to quantify the causal gap.

6. Estimation

With the causal question defined, the data assessed, the study designed, and identifiability established, the final step is to choose an estimator that targets the statistical estimand under a realistic statistical model, using IPW, g-methods, or doubly robust extensions.
