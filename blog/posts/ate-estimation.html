2025-03-10 Causal Inference

Average Treatment Effect Estimation under Unconfoundedness

How do we estimate the causal effect of a treatment when we can't randomise? This post builds up from potential outcomes to doubly robust estimators, with the key assumptions spelled out at each step.

Potential Outcomes

For each unit $i$, we define two potential outcomes: $Y_i(1)$ under treatment, and $Y_i(0)$ under control. The individual treatment effect is $\tau_i = Y_i(1) - Y_i(0)$, but we only ever observe one of these — the fundamental problem of causal inference.

The quantity we usually target is the Average Treatment Effect:

$$\text{ATE} = \mathbb{E}[\tau_i] = \mathbb{E}[Y_i(1) - Y_i(0)]$$

The Unconfoundedness Assumption

In an observational study, treatment $D_i$ is not randomly assigned. To identify the ATE, we need to assume that, conditional on observed covariates $X_i$, treatment assignment is as good as random:

$$(Y_i(0), Y_i(1)) \perp\!\!\!\perp D_i \mid X_i$$

Key intuition: once we control for everything in $X_i$ that jointly affects treatment and outcome, there's no remaining confounding. This is a strong assumption — and untestable from data alone.

We also require overlap (or positivity): every unit has some positive probability of receiving either treatment:

$$0 < e(x) < 1 \quad \forall x \in \mathcal{X}$$

where $e(x) = P(D_i = 1 \mid X_i = x)$ is the propensity score.

Doubly Robust Estimation

The Augmented Inverse Probability Weighted (AIPW) estimator combines outcome modelling and propensity score weighting. It is doubly robust: consistent if either the outcome model $\hat{\mu}(d, x)$ or the propensity score $\hat{e}(x)$ is correctly specified.

$$\hat{\tau}^{\text{AIPW}} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \left[ \hat{\mu}(1, X_i) - \hat{\mu}(0, X_i) + \frac{D_i\bigl(Y_i - \hat{\mu}(1,X_i)\bigr)}{\hat{e}(X_i)} - \frac{(1-D_i)\bigl(Y_i - \hat{\mu}(0,X_i)\bigr)}{1 - \hat{e}(X_i)} \right]$$

The first two terms are the direct regression adjustment. The last two terms are augmentation corrections that remove bias when one of the models is misspecified.
