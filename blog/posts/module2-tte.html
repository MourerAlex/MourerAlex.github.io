2025-03-20 Causal Inference

Target Trial Emulation

Designing observational studies as if they were trials.

1. Why Target Trial Emulation?

Randomized Clinical Trials (RCTs) are the gold standard for estimating the causal effect of a treatment on an outcome. Their primary advantage lies in randomization itself: by design, the groups being compared are exchangeable at baseline, and any difference in outcomes can be attributed to the treatment strategy.

Yet RCTs are not always feasible. Ethical constraints, prohibitive costs, the need for timely evidence (as was the case with COVID-19 vaccines), or the rarity of the condition under study may make a randomized trial impractical or impossible. In these situations, observational data—electronic health records, claims databases, registries—become essential.

The problem is that observational analyses are vulnerable to a class of biases that do not arise in well-conducted trials. Some of these biases, such as confounding and measurement error, are widely recognized. Others are more insidious—design-related biases—that result not from the data itself but from how the analyst sets up the study: when follow-up starts, how treatment groups are defined, who is included, and when.

Key insight

Many flawed observational analyses are not wrong because the data was bad. They are wrong because the analyst did not think carefully enough about what trial they were trying to emulate. Immortal time bias, selection bias, and misclassification of treatment strategies are all consequences of a misalignment between the study design and the causal question (Hernán et al., 2016; Hernán et al., 2025).

Target Trial Emulation (TTE) was developed as a framework to prevent these fundamental design errors (Hernán et al., 2022). The idea is simple but powerful: before analyzing any observational data, explicitly specify the hypothetical randomized trial—the target trial—that would answer your causal question, and then design the observational analysis to emulate it as closely as possible.

The framework forces the analyst to make all design choices transparent and linked to the components of a trial protocol: eligibility criteria, treatment strategies, treatment assignment, follow-up, outcomes, causal contrast, and statistical analysis plan. By doing so, it turns implicit assumptions into explicit ones that can be scrutinized, debated, and improved.

What TTE does not solve

TTE is a design framework, not a statistical method. It prevents design-related biases (immortal time, selection at time zero, misclassification of strategies), but it does not eliminate confounding, measurement error, or missing data. These require additional statistical methods—many of which are covered in other modules of this series (IPW, G-formula, AIPW, TMLE).

2. A Motivating Example

To make the discussion concrete, consider a large randomized phase 3 trial designed to evaluate the impact of a new drug (Drug A) versus an active comparator (Drug B) on kidney outcomes in patients with type 2 diabetes (T2D) and chronic kidney disease (CKD). Suppose this multi-center, parallel-group trial found that Drug A reduced the risk of kidney outcomes and cardiovascular-related death compared to Drug B.

Running example (used across this module)

A clinical team wants to evaluate whether Drug A also benefits a specific subgroup of patients—those with moderate kidney function (eGFR between 60 and 75 mL/min/1.73 m²)—using real-world data from a large healthcare database. The original trial was not powered for this subgroup analysis, and random variation is the most probable explanation for any apparent subgroup differences, limiting actionable clinical insights (ISIS-2 Collaborative Group, 1988).

The research question can then be defined as: "What is the effect of Drug A versus Drug B on kidney outcomes in patients with T2D, CKD, and eGFR in [60; 75] mL/min/1.73 m²?"

This is a causal question. Answering it from observational data means we need to think carefully about what trial we would run, and then design our analysis to emulate it. The rest of this module shows how to do exactly that.

3. The Target Trial Protocol

An advantage of TTE is that it produces an explicitly specified target trial protocol—analogous to a real trial protocol—that forces all design decisions to be made upfront. The protocol has the following components (Hernán et al., 2022):

Protocol component Description
Eligibility criteria Who can enter the study? What baseline characteristics are required?
Treatment strategies What are the treatment strategies being compared?
Treatment assignment How are individuals assigned to strategies?
Start & end of follow-up When does follow-up begin and end for each individual?
Outcomes What events are we measuring?
Causal contrast What effect are we estimating? (e.g., intention-to-treat, per-protocol)
Statistical analysis plan What statistical methods will be used to estimate the contrast?

While eligibility criteria, outcomes, and the causal contrast may closely resemble those of the target trial, other components—like treatment assignment and the start of follow-up—must be emulated using study design and statistical methods (see Sections 5 and 6).

3.1 Causal effects and counterfactual outcomes

Causal effects are fundamentally rooted in the concept of counterfactual outcomes: the outcome that would have been observed under each treatment strategy. Among these, only the one corresponding to the strategy actually received is factual; the rest are counterfactual (Hernán, 2004).

For a binary treatment setting, each individual has two potential outcomes: $Y^{a=1}$ (outcome under Drug A) and $Y^{a=0}$ (outcome under Drug B). Causal effects are defined as contrasts of these counterfactual outcomes. While only one outcome is observed per individual, average causal effects can, under certain conditions, be identified from data.

Identifiability conditions

Estimating average causal effects from observational data requires three core assumptions (see also Module 0): exchangeability (same risk in both arms at time zero, conditional on measured confounders), positivity (a non-zero probability of being assigned to each treatment level for every combination of confounders), and consistency (well-defined treatment strategies). For details, refer to Chapters 1–3 of Hernán and Robins (2024).

In our motivating example, the causal question becomes a what if question: "What would be the $X$-week risk if everybody had always been treated with Drug B?" versus "What would be the $X$-week risk if everybody had always been treated with Drug A?" This framing makes explicit that we are comparing two counterfactual worlds.

3.2 Treatment strategies and effect measures

A common misconception is that trials compare treatments. In fact, they compare treatment strategies (Hernán et al., 2017). In a clinical trial, individuals assigned to a particular treatment arm must be allowed to discontinue, modify, or switch treatment when clinically indicated. This distinction matters because it determines which causal contrast we estimate.

The effect of assignment to a treatment strategy—the intention-to-treat (ITT) effect—is generally favored in RCTs for its simplicity and its conservative nature (it biases towards the null when there is non-adherence). Conversely, for safety studies, this conservatism is undesirable (Murray et al., 2019).

On the other hand, per-protocol analysis considers static treatment strategies such as "always treated." It is preferred in observational studies when there is substantial non-adherence or loss to follow-up, because an intention-to-treat approach could result in significant exposure misclassification (Hernán et al., 2016; Hernán and Robins, 2024, Sections 22.1–22.2).

A note on hazard ratios

For effect measures, absolute risks, their differences, and their ratios are usually preferred for causal interpretation (Murray et al., 2019). Hazard ratios have a built-in selection bias, first described in Hernán (2010) and further developed in Martinussen et al. (2018), Stensrud et al. (2019), and Martinussen (2022). This does not mean hazard ratios should never be used, but their interpretation as causal parameters requires care.

3.3 A note on non-interventional exposures

The target trial should align plausibly with a trial that could technically be conducted. When studying the impact of a non-interventional factor like a comorbidity (e.g., dyslipidemia, obesity) or a biomarker (e.g., weight), defining a treatment strategy based on the presence of the comorbidity or on biomarker values is not feasible. In such cases, the TTE framework may not facilitate effective communication, and additional frameworks—such as mediation analysis—may be needed (Aris et al., 2021; VanderWeele, 2011).

4. From Ideal Trial to Pragmatic Trial

In TTE, the ideal clinical trial—the hypothetical trial one would conduct if there were no constraints—is rarely the trial one aims to emulate. Differences in objectives, feasibility, and the limitations of available data mean that the analyst must work through an intermediate step: defining a pragmatic clinical trial.

Pragmatic clinical trial

A trial that examines interventions under circumstances that approach real-world practice, with more heterogeneous patient populations, possibly less-standardised treatment protocols, and delivery in routine clinical settings as opposed to a research environment (Anes et al., 2024).

The pragmatic trial is a realistic trial that one could envisage running, and it is exactly the trial one will target when applying TTE. Guidelines on implementing pragmatic trials have been reviewed by Murray et al. (2019).

Figure 2.2 — The three-step process. The ideal trial is the best version of the trial we could envisage. The pragmatic trial is what is feasible given real-world data constraints. The emulation maps the pragmatic trial to the observational data.

In our motivating example, the three steps might involve: (1) the ideal trial is the original RCT restricted to the eGFR subgroup, (2) the pragmatic trial relaxes placebo control (using an active comparator instead, since a placebo arm is unlikely to be feasible in the data), may modify endpoints due to data availability (e.g., replacing cause-specific death with all-cause mortality), and may exclude certain eligibility criteria that cannot be measured in the database, and (3) the emulation addresses further operational differences (e.g., defining follow-up end as the end of insurance enrollment, handling operational definitions of outcomes).

Communication to decision makers

The pragmatic trial design can be characterized as the trial design currently being targeted, yet emulated with observational data. Focusing on the pragmatic trial design when engaging with decision makers may enhance understanding and facilitate discussions, without confusing them with operational implementation details that are specific to the emulation step.

A note on the estimand framework

The estimand framework (EuropeanMedicalAgency, 2020) has been introduced for RCTs, providing clear delineation of treatment strategies and intercurrent events (e.g., treatment discontinuation), and the methods for managing them (Kahan et al., 2024). Combining the estimand framework with TTE has the potential to enhance internal communication and transparency. For an example of real-world data application of the estimand framework together with the Causal Roadmap framework, refer to Dang et al. (2023).

5. Emulating Randomization

In a randomized trial, the time of randomization marks the attainment of three key components:

# Component What it achieves
1 Form comparable groups Ensures that any prognostic differences between groups are due solely to the treatment.
2 Assign treatment Marks the initiation of the treatment strategy.
3 Start follow-up Establishes the starting point (time zero) for the trial.

Achieving component 1 relies on statistical methods (covered in Modules 0–1 and later modules). Components 2 and 3 are more closely tied to the study design—specifically the definition of time zero relative to treatment initiation. When these components are not properly synchronized, design-related biases emerge.

5.1 Emulation failures

The main challenges when emulating a trial were outlined in Hernán et al. (2016) and can be summarized as four emulation failures:

Failure Description
(i) Establishing time zero
(ii) Adjusting for adherence when treatment strategies are sustained over time
(iii) Assigning individuals to a unique treatment strategy at time zero
(iv) Salvaging the analysis when too few individuals initiate a treatment strategy of interest

Addressing these challenges requires careful selection of both design and statistical methods, since aligning treatment assignment and the start of follow-up is inherently connected to forming comparable groups over time and emulating sustained treatment strategies.

5.2 Immortal time: a structural description of the biases

A particularly insidious consequence of failing to properly synchronize eligibility, treatment assignment, and the start of follow-up is the generation of immortal time—a period included in the analysis during which a person, by definition, cannot develop the event of interest (Hernán et al., 2025). Survival analyses with immortal time yield biased effect estimates, as has been widely documented.

Key insight

Individuals are not truly immortal. Rather, the existence of immortal time in analyses of observational data results from a failure to correctly emulate the target trial. Specifically, it arises when the eligibility criteria and treatment assignment are not synchronized at the start of follow-up (Hernán et al., 2025).

Hernán et al. (2025) identify two distinct structural mechanisms that generate immortal time:

5.2.1 Immortal time due to selection

Immortal time due to selection appears when an eligibility criterion is (incorrectly) applied after the start of follow-up. Consider a trial where individuals are randomly assigned to treatment at time zero. If the investigators accidentally deleted the first 3 months of follow-up data for every individual, anyone who died during those 3 months would disappear from the dataset. The resulting survival curves would be flat during this period —all individuals appear to have "survived" because those who didn't are missing from the data. This creates a 3-month immortal period.

In observational studies, this situation arises when investigators restrict the analysis to individuals who have completed a certain duration of follow-up. This selection induces differential exclusion of the most susceptible individuals in each treatment group, biasing the comparison. The resulting selection bias with differential depletion of susceptibles is sometimes called "prevalent user bias."

Figure 2.3 — Causal diagram for immortal time due to selection. Treatment $A_0$ is assigned at time zero. An eligibility criterion $Y_3$ is applied after assignment. $U$ (e.g., susceptibility) is a common cause of $Y_3$ and the outcome $Y_{12}$. Selecting on $Y_3$ opens a biasing path.

What to do: Ensure that all eligibility criteria are defined at time zero, so that no selection occurs after treatment assignment. This rule is naturally followed by explicitly emulating a target trial based on data for all eligible individuals from the time of treatment assignment.

5.2.2 Immortal time due to misclassification

Immortal time also appears when misclassifying individuals into a treatment group that differs from the one they were assigned to (in randomized trials) or should have been assigned to (in observational studies). This type of immortal time may arise when some treatment strategies under study cannot be distinguished at time zero.

Consider a target trial comparing three strategies: (1) never receive treatment, (2) start treatment immediately and stop after 1 month, (3) start treatment immediately and stop after 2 months. Strategies 2 and 3 are indistinguishable at time zero. If an investigator constructs treatment groups by looking at what actually happened during follow-up (e.g., assigning $Z^*=2$ to individuals who completed 2 months of treatment), anyone who died before completing 2 months would be misclassified into $Z^*=0$ or $Z^*=1$. Individuals in the $Z^*=2$ group are thus "immortal" until they complete 2 months of treatment, making that group look artificially better.

The classic example of this bias is the original heart transplant studies, where time zero was set at the time patients became eligible (accepted as candidates), but patients who died while waiting for a transplant were assigned to the "no transplant" group.

What to do: Several approaches prevent this type of immortal time:

Approaches to avoid misclassification-induced immortal time

1. Change the causal question to compare strategies that are distinguishable at time zero. For example, instead of comparing "transplant" vs. "no transplant," compare "transplant today" vs. "no transplant ever" among patients who have survived a fixed waiting period.

2. Cloning, censoring, and weighting. Create "clones" of each individual—one assigned to each strategy they are compatible with at baseline. Each clone is censored when their data becomes incompatible with their assigned strategy. Inverse probability of censoring weighting (IPCW) is then used to adjust for the informative censoring introduced by the cloning step (Hernán et al., 2022).

3. The plug-in g-formula, which requires estimating an iterated conditional expectation or the joint distribution of the time-varying treatment, outcome, and confounders (Hernán and Robins, 2024, Chapter 21).

5.3 The TARGET reporting guideline

The importance of properly specifying and reporting all components of a target trial emulation has led to the development of a formal consensus-based reporting standard: the TARGET (Transparent Reporting of Observational Studies Emulating a Target Trial) statement (Cashin et al., 2025). Published simultaneously in The BMJ and JAMA, TARGET provides a 21-item checklist organized around the typical structure of a research report.

TARGET — the new state of the art for reporting

TARGET is the first formal guideline specifically designed for reporting studies that emulate a target trial. It was developed through systematic review, international expert consensus (18 panelists from 6 countries), and piloting with over 100 stakeholders. The checklist is freely available at target-guideline.org.

While the methods described in this module teach you how to design and analyze a TTE study, TARGET tells you what to report when you write it up. Using both together ensures that studies are both methodologically sound and transparently communicated.

A key feature of TARGET is its dual-column structure: for each protocol component, the checklist asks the analyst to report both the target trial specification (what the trial would look like) and the emulation (how each component was mapped to the observational data). This mirrors the two-step logic of TTE itself.

TARGET section Selected items
Abstract Identify the study as a TTE; report data sources; summarize key assumptions and findings.
Introduction Describe background, causal question, and rationale for emulating a target trial.
Methods — Specification Specify the target trial protocol: eligibility, treatment strategies, assignment, follow-up, outcomes, causal contrasts, identifying assumptions, analysis plan.
Methods — Emulation For each protocol component, describe how it was operationalized with the data.
Results Flow diagram, baseline data, follow-up, missing data, outcomes, effect estimates, sensitivity analyses.
Discussion Interpretation of findings; limitations considering differences between target trial and emulation.

6. Methods to Emulate Trials and Their Related Causal Questions

In this section, we explore approaches that address the emulation failures (i)–(iv) described in Section 5, and discuss how appropriate designs and statistical methods achieve valid group comparisons, align treatment assignment with the start of follow-up, and handle sustained treatment strategies.

6.1 Sequentially randomized experiments

Emulating trials in practice often involves comparing drug exposures where the time-varying nature of treatment implies the potential presence of time-varying confounders that, if not adjusted for, can prevent the experiment from being sequentially randomized.

6.1.1 G-methods

Sequentially randomized experiments involve random treatment assignment at each time, dependent solely on the values of prior covariate and treatment history. This defines sequential exchangeability for the outcome, ensuring that treatment arms at each time are exchangeable with respect to the outcome, given prior covariate history and observed treatment history compatible with the strategy (see Hernán and Robins, 2024, Chapter 19, Section 3).

When addressing time-varying treatments, the challenge of treatment-confounder feedback arises. Traditional regression models (e.g., the Cox proportional hazards model) may introduce bias when time-varying confounders are influenced by past treatment exposure, as classical adjustment can lead to adjusting away some of the treatment effect. This is analogous to mediation: treatment affects a time-varying confounder (an intermediate), which in turn affects the outcome. Conditioning on this intermediate blocks part of the causal pathway.

To mitigate this bias, g-methods are required: the g-formula, inverse probability (IP) weighting, g-estimation, and their doubly-robust generalizations (combinations of IP weighting and g-formula, or the Longitudinal-Targeted Maximum Likelihood Estimator, L-TMLE). See Hernán and Robins (2024), Chapter 21.

Not optional

Given the untestable nature of treatment-confounder feedback assumptions, the use of g-methods is not optional but mandatory when time-varying confounding is present.

6.1.2 Treatment switch or treatment discontinuation

Non-adherence occurs when study participants fail to adhere to the assigned treatment strategy. In real-world data, the most prevalent types of non-adherence are:

Type Description
Treatment switch Switching from one study drug to another (e.g., from Drug A to Drug B).
Treatment discontinuation Stopping any treatment completely, or intensifying treatment with additional drugs.

Treatment discontinuation is handled by artificially censoring patients at the time of discontinuation, followed by adjustment for the informative censoring through IP weighting of artificial censoring (IPACW) (Murray et al., 2019).

Treatment switch can be handled via g-methods. If the strongest form of sequential exchangeability holds (as well as positivity and consistency), g-methods allow for validly estimating the causal effect of any treatment strategy. The main difference between IPACW and g-estimation is that IPACW discards a patient's data once they deviate from the strategies of interest, making it more robust against model misspecification but less statistically efficient. G-estimation of structural models uses this data to estimate causal effects, making it more efficient but relying on the assumption that the structural model is correct (Hernán et al., 2006).

6.2 Study designs

We now outline three distinct designs for emulating trials, falling under two primary categories:

Category Design Key reference
New-user designs Active-New User Design (ACNU) Lund et al., 2015
Cloning Censoring Weighting (CCW) Hernán, 2018
Prevalent-user designs Sequential Nested Trials (SNT) Hernán et al., 2008

6.2.1 Active-New User Design (ACNU)

The ACNU design aims to replicate the structure of a head-to-head RCT. It involves assembling cohorts of patients newly prescribed Drug A and patients newly prescribed Drug B. The "active comparator" element reduces bias by focusing on individuals necessitating treatment and without contraindications. The "new user" component ensures a standardized starting point for follow-up, aligning individuals at treatment initiation (Lund et al., 2015).

ACNU causal question

"What would be the $X$-weeks risk if, at the time of trial start, everybody had initiated and always been treated with Drug A?" versus "What would be the $X$-weeks risk if, at the time of trial start, everybody had initiated and always been treated with Drug B?"

ACNU addresses emulation failures (i) and (iii) by aligning time zero and treatment assignment. Treatment discontinuation can be addressed with IPACW (Section 6.1), and treatment switch can be handled with g-methods if assumptions are met.

However, the effect estimated with ACNU pertains to a new-user population, which may differ from the population in trials that include prevalent users.

Note on statistical efficiency

Statistical methods yield reliable estimates only if there is sufficient data support. In the ACNU design, if very few patients initiate one of the study drugs within the follow-up window, the analysis extrapolates without adequate data support, creating positivity issues and inconsistent estimates. This motivates more complex designs when data are sparse.

6.2.2 Cloning Censoring Weighting (CCW)

CCW is a method used to emulate a trial with a grace period—a defined period at the start of follow-up during which the treatment strategy can differ from that employed during the rest of follow-up. For example, one might allow individuals to initiate Drug A within $X$ months after starting Drug B.

CCW causal question

"What would be the $X$-weeks risk if, at the time of trial start, everybody had initiated within $X$ months and thereafter always been treated with Drug A?" versus "What would be the $X$-weeks risk if, at the time of trial start, everybody had initiated and always been treated with Drug B?"

At time zero, since everyone is compatible with both the "initiated within $X$ months" and "always treated" strategies, it is not possible to assign a single strategy to any participant until they switch. The solution is to clone each patient into both treatment strategies at baseline, censoring them when their data becomes incompatible with their assigned strategy, and then applying IPCW to adjust for the informative censoring (Hernán et al., 2022).

One issue with CCW is that reformulating the emulation with a grace period can result in double counting of events during the grace period, diluting the effect. Additionally, after the grace period ends, many switchers will be artificially censored, resulting in a loss of efficiency. As noted by Hernán (2018), an alternative that eliminates immortal time bias, estimates absolute risks, and adequately handles treatment-confounder feedback is the g-formula.

6.2.3 Sequential Nested Trials (SNT)

The Sequential Nested Trials design solves emulation failures (i), (iii), and (iv) by aligning the start of follow-up, treatment assignment, and capturing all possible users eligible in each strategy—both prevalent and new users.

In our motivating example, this approach could emulate a trial where prevalent use of Drug B is no longer an exclusion criterion. Eligibility criteria are satisfied at multiple points in time for every Drug B user until the initiation of Drug A. There are two unbiased options for handling multiple eligible times: select a single eligible time (as in ACNU or a random eligible time), or select all possible choices of time zero for all participants—the SNT design (Hernán and Robins, 2016; Hernán and Robins, 2024, Section 22.4).

SNT causal question

"What would be the $X$-weeks risk if, at the time of trial start, everybody had initiated and always been treated with Drug A?" versus "What would be the $X$-weeks risk if, at the time of trial start, everybody had initiated or stayed and always been treated with Drug B?"

The SNT is arguably the most statistically efficient design as it utilizes all available data (patients and outcomes). The ability to align treatment strategies for all eligible patients at time zero results in more efficient estimation, especially during the early follow-up periods (Keogh et al., 2023).

Prevalence-incidence (Neyman) bias

Because the SNT includes both prevalent and new users, comparing groups with unbalanced drug history may lead to an unfair comparison if drug history influences the risk of the outcome. The SNT does not address this bias unless the history is balanced in each group at the start of follow-up. Consequently, it is necessary to account for drug history in the statistical analysis, and left truncation must be appropriately handled.

Please note that the Prevalent-New User Design (PNUD; Suissa et al., 2017) is also a prevalent user design. It performs sequential exact-matching on a single covariate: study drug history. Subsequently, a second matching (e.g., on propensity score) is carried out within each matched subset. Compared to sequential matching with time-varying covariates (Lu, 2005), the novelty lies in enforcing sequential exact matching on drug history before the propensity score matching. However, this approach answers a different causal question than a clinical trial—it examines the impact of treatment within those who have received the treatment—and may not be directly applicable within the TTE framework (Young et al., 2024).
